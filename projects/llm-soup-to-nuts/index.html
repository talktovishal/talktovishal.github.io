Learning by doing

LLM Soup to Nuts.

I'm writing this for one reader in particular: a curious person with basic math background, who keeps hearing about AI and ChatGPT and wants to actually understand how they work. My goal is to start small, learn by building from the basics. I'll introduce each piece slowly and build it up. If you can read and you're willing to tinker, you will build small, working versions of these ideas yourself, right here in your browser.

Here is how I think about it. I don't want you to memorize buzzwords. I want you to build the smallest honest version of each idea, change something, and watch what happens. We start with the goal of building a model to predict the next word. We do that by borrowing evidence from similar words, training a tiny neural network, splitting text into tokens, letting words pay attention to each other, and finally seeing how the whole thing scales up. Don't worry if these terms are alien to you. We will learn them all! Every time the math appears, it is there to explain something you can see on the screen, never the other way around.

My promise to you: if an idea matters, you will get to see it, change something, watch the result, and understand it in complete depth. Let me know if I fail to deliver on that promise by giving me feedback using the link at the bottom of the page.

Why I built it this way

Build from basics, no unnecessary complexity.

I lean on deliberately small examples because small models let you see the whole machine at once. A table of words and their counts, a single vector update, a softmax, or an attention map is so much easier to trust once you have watched the numbers move with your own eyes.

I also work hard to keep the chapters connected. Everything starts with one simple question, what word comes next, and that same question keeps coming back as the machinery changes. That throughline is what makes the jump from n-grams to neural networks to Transformers feel like a series of small incremental steps instead of a pile of disconnected terms. Once again, don't worry about the terms, we will get to them soon :-).

Chapter 1 · Build a tiny next-word predictor

This is where it all starts. You'll build the simplest model that guesses the next word just by counting which words tend to follow which, the same basic trick your phone keyboard uses when it suggests your next word. Along the way you'll meet tokens and n-grams and a couple of friendly ways to measure how good a guess really is.

Chapter 2 · When counting words is no longer sufficient

Plain counting works great until the exact phrase you need has never shown up before. You'll run straight into that wall on purpose, then climb over it by giving every word its own little list of numbers, so that words used in similar ways can share what they have learned. This is the first real step from simple shallow learning to what we call as deep learning :-).

Chapter 3 · Tokenization

When we eat something we take small bits of the food, correct? In the same way when the model sees a big book it chops the text into pieces called tokens. It sounds boring, but how you cut matters a lot: it changes cost, speed, and even fairness. You'll train real tokenizers and watch the same sentence get split in surprisingly different ways.

Chapter 4 · The math that makes a model guess

This is the friendly math chapter, and nothing is assumed. One piece at a time, you'll build the small toolkit a model actually uses, probability, vectors, a dot product, softmax, and the gentle nudge called a gradient, and then train a tiny neural network right in your browser. Every idea shows up only when it solves a problem you can see.

Chapter 5 · Transformers and attention Coming soon

This is the big one, the idea behind nearly every AI tool you have heard of. You'll build attention from scratch, the trick that lets every word look at every other word at once, and then load a real, working language model right into your browser to watch it predict and write. By the end, the word Transformer won't feel like magic anymore. Once again, don't worry you will learn it all :-)

Chapter 6 · Pretraining LLMs Coming soon

Finally, you'll zoom out to how these models get trained on enormous amounts of text. You'll play with the real tradeoffs that AI labs argue about: what goal to train toward, how to clean messy web data, and whether to spend your budget on a bigger model or more data. It is the same tiny loop from Chapter 4, just blown up to an enormous scale.
