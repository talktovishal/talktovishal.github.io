Learning by doing

When counting words is no longer sufficient.

In Chapter 1 we built a real little language model out of nothing but counts. It worked! But it was also a strict librarian: if it had never seen the exact phrase before, it mostly just shrugged.

Here is the moment that nagged at me. Suppose our text has seen the cat slept but never the dog slept. You and I read that and instantly think, "well, of course a dog can sleep too" - cats and dogs turn up in the same kinds of places. But our count table keeps cat and dog in completely separate boxes, so every bit of evidence we gathered for one is useless to the other. Remember our motto from Chapter 1, you are known by the company you keep? Wouldn't it be lovely if the model knew that too?

So this chapter chases exactly that wish. We still ask the same down-to-earth question from Chapter 1 - what word might come next? - but we hand the model a new superpower: a sense that some words are alike. First we will watch the old count table fail. Then we will build a new kind of evidence, slowly and by hand, out of the company each word keeps, and then train it live in your browser. By the end, even remembering a clue from far away in a sentence will feel less mysterious.

Guiding question: can a model lean on evidence from similar words, not just identical word windows? That one idea is the bridge from the n-grams of Chapter 1 to the neural language models that power modern LLMs.

Before we dive in, a few prerequisites are in order. This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1, and every new idea is introduced slowly the moment we need it. Still, a little comfort with the following will make the ride smoother. Please use the links as quick refreshers if anything feels new.

  • Chapter 1. We reuse tokens, counts, and the question P(next | context). If "what word comes next" already feels natural, you are ready.
  • A list of numbers (a vector). The whole chapter rests on giving each word a short row of numbers, like [0.42, -0.10, 0.31]. No prior vector-math background is needed; we build the few operations we use right where they show up.
  • Multiplying and adding. Our one workhorse is the dot product: multiply two lists position by position, then add the results. We will do it by hand before any code runs.
  • Reading a little code. Just like last chapter, short runnable snippets you can change and re-run. Reading them is enough; writing any is optional.

What you do not need yet: no calculus, no matrix algebra, and no neural-network experience. Those arrive in Chapter 3, right when we put them to work.

1. Limits of n-grams

The count table breaks when exact evidence disappears.

Let's start by feeling the problem for ourselves, because it is too easy to wave away in words. The n-gram from Chapter 1 was a wonderful first model: count the word windows, then lean on those counts to guess what comes next. But it is a very literal creature. If it has never seen the exact context, it cannot borrow a hunch from a similar context it has seen - even when, to us, the two are obviously cousins.

Try the small lab below. Type one two-word phrase per line. The model first hunts for that exact two-word phrase. If it cannot find it, it does the only thing it knows: it drops the first word and falls back to asking what usually followed just the last word. Watch how often the precise phrase simply is not there.

Start simple. Compare brave queen and small rabbit. Neither exact two-word phrase appears in this Alice corpus, so in both cases the model shrugs and drops the first word. But look at what is left: the fallback now leans entirely on the last word, so queen points toward said while rabbit points toward hole. The very adjective we cared about - brave, small - was thrown away completely. Useful, but blunt.

2. Neural probabilistic language models

First, give each word a small row of numbers.

The section above let us feel the problem: a count table only trusts exact phrases. If it has seen the cat slept but not the dog slept, it has no built-in reason to let cat lend a hand to dog. The fix, introduced by neural language models, is lovely to state: stop treating words as separate boxes, and give each one a learned representation instead.

"Representation" can sound fancy, so let me make it plain. A word vector is just a short list of numbers that stands in for one word - that is the whole idea. At the very start those numbers are basically random noise. Then training gently nudges them, over and over, and here is the quiet magic: words that keep showing up in similar company get nudged toward similar numbers. The company a word keeps slowly becomes the numbers a word carries.

Our browser demo uses 36 numbers per word. There is nothing sacred about 36 - it is a knob we pick before training, the kind of choice we call a hyperparameter. Real systems often use hundreds. We choose 36 so the training still finishes in a few seconds in your browser, yet it is rich enough to show real neighborhood patterns. Do you already feel the tradeoff? More numbers can capture more nuance, but cost more to train and store.

A vector is a row of learned numbers

The three rows below are just a sketch to fix the picture in your mind - the browser will learn its own numbers from your text. Do not read meaning into any single value; the useful part is simply the shape: one word, one row, a handful of small numbers.

dog [0.42, -0.10, 0.31, 0.08, ...]
cat [0.39, -0.12, 0.29, 0.11, ...]
table [-0.05, 0.33, -0.18, 0.22, ...]

If dog and cat appear near many of the same words, training can make their rows more alike. The model is not given labels like "animal." It learns from word neighborhoods.

What skip-gram trains on

Skip-gram plays a simple guessing game. It picks a center word and asks: which words really showed up nearby? Those genuine pairs are the ones to reward. Then negative sampling tosses in a few made-up pairs that never occurred, so the model also learns what does not belong together. Pull real pairs close, push fake pairs apart - that is the whole trick.

alice heard the rabbit nearby
center word rabbit
real nearby pairs rabbit -> the rabbit -> nearby raise the score
made-up pairs rabbit -> king rabbit -> table lower the score

This is a simplified redraw of the Word2Vec skip-gram idea. The original Word2Vec paper also contrasts skip-gram with CBOW, which predicts a center word from its surrounding words.

What one training step does

Every word starts life as a small list of random numbers. Training is just the patient work of making those numbers less random, one tiny correction at a time. Here is a single step: the model grabs a center word such as rabbit and one word from its nearby window, such as watch, and checks how well their current numbers agree.

  1. Look up the current vector for rabbit.
  2. Look up the current vector for watch.
  3. Take a dot product. This asks, "Do these two rows point in a similar direction?"
  4. Turn that score into a probability between 0 and 1 with sigmoid, an S-shaped squashing function.
  5. Compare the probability with the answer: real pair or made-up pair.
  6. Nudge the numbers so the next score is a little less wrong.

One nudge is tiny. Thousands of nudges create the pattern. One full sweep through all of those word pairs is called an epoch, and the training-depth control simply sets how many sweeps to run. That is why the loss trace matters: it shows the repeated nudges are making the training task easier for the model.

score = center vector · context vector probability real = sigmoid(score) loss = surprise about the right answer

Why fake pairs are useful

If rabbit → nearby really appeared, training nudges the vectors so that score goes up. If rabbit → table was a made-up negative example, training nudges the score down. After many passes, words with similar neighborhoods receive similar nudges, so their final lists of numbers become more alike.

This is why nearby words mean similar usage in this corpus. They are not guaranteed to be synonyms.

One real nudge, with numbers

illustrative

Let's do a single step by hand, so the "random numbers get adjusted" part stops being hand-wavy. To keep it readable, imagine just three numbers per word instead of 36, and suppose rabbit and watch really did appear near each other, so the right answer for this pair is "real."

rabbit (before)[0.20, -0.10, 0.05]
watch[0.30, 0.00, 0.10]
score = 0.20×0.30 + (-0.10)×0.00 + 0.05×0.10 = 0.065 probability real = sigmoid(0.065) ≈ 0.516 nudge = learningRate × (1 − 0.516) = 0.035 × 0.484 ≈ 0.017 rabbit += nudge × watch → [0.205, -0.10, 0.052]

That nudge slid rabbit a step toward watch, and the same step pulls watch back toward rabbit. Recompute the score and it has risen from 0.065 to about 0.067, so next time the model is a touch more sure these two belong together. A made-up pair runs the exact same machinery with the answer flipped to "not real," gently pushing the two rows apart instead.

One nudge is invisibly small. The whole trick is repetition: thousands of real pairs tug look-alike words together while thousands of made-up pairs shove unrelated words apart, and the random starting numbers slowly settle into a meaningful shape. The live run does this with 36 numbers per word, but each step is exactly the arithmetic above.

Now choose the text and train

This is the fun part: pick a corpus, decide how much of it to use, and press the button to watch the numbers actually move. The default uses both Alice books, Wonderland and Looking-Glass, so there are extra repeated characters and scenes for the map to organize, and it still trains in just a few seconds. The other books and the balanced mix are there if you want to compare different worlds. More epochs can lower loss, but in a small browser model they can also over-specialize, so judge a run by the neighbor evidence, not just the final loss number. The browser thins out the most common glue words and reshuffles the pairs on every pass, so content words land closer together; it still uses common words as context evidence.

As it runs, keep an eye on the word-pair panel: those cosine bars creeping upward are similar-usage words sliding toward each other, one tiny nudge at a time.

Good experiments: the default both-books Alice already covers queen, croquet, tweedledum, and humpty; switch to Pride and Prejudice for elizabeth and darcy, or Sherlock Holmes for holmes and watson. When you switch corpora, the meaning of "nearby" changes with the text.

3. Word embeddings

Now inspect the word vectors you trained.

That trained list of numbers for a word has a name: a word embedding. In this chapter rabbit gets one embedding, queen gets one, and so on. Ours are static embeddings - the same word carries the same row of numbers everywhere it appears. (Hold on to that word "static"; in the Transformer chapter we will meet embeddings that change with context, and that contrast is the whole point.)

The browser trains vectors for up to 260 focus words from your text, and it deliberately keeps the interesting ones: characters, names, places, and other content words. It quietly drops most of the high-frequency glue - the, of, and - because a map crowded with those would teach us nothing about meaning.

Two famous methods popularized this idea. Word2Vec, which is the recipe we train here, learns from local prediction, while GloVe learns from a big table of how often words co-occur. Different recipes, same practical payoff: words become learned numbers, and nearby vectors tend to reflect related usage. Notice I said usage, not meaning. In the Alice corpus, queen may sit near other characters or shouted-dialogue words simply because that is how the book uses her - not because of any dictionary definition.

Read the map gently

The model has 36 numbers per word, but the screen has only two axes. PCA is a drawing method that squeezes the 36-number vectors into an x and y position. It is useful for a first look, but it can hide some distances and exaggerate others.

Trust neighbors more than the picture

The neighbor list compares the original 36-number vectors with cosine similarity. That means it asks whether two rows point in a similar direction. It uses the full trained vectors, not the flattened map view.

Try analogies, but stay skeptical

Classic examples like king - man + woman came from much larger training runs. Our browser model is intentionally small. Vector math can still be interesting, but failed or strange answers are part of the lesson.

Read this like evidence, not magic. If a corpus uses two words in similar places, they may become neighbors. If the corpus is small, genre-specific, or biased, the vectors can be odd or biased too. Use the "Why these neighbors?" box to inspect the context clues behind the list.

What "good" looks like here. On a single short book, expect clean neighbors mainly for words that show up often and in distinctive places: character names, pronouns, and courtesy titles like mr and miss. Rare words will look noisy, and a textbook analogy such as king − man + woman may simply miss. That is not a failure of the page; it is what a small corpus can and cannot support. For sharper neighbors, train on more text or pick a corpus where your target words are common.

4. Sequence models before Transformers

What if the useful clue is far away?

Embeddings fixed one problem - similar words can finally help each other - but a quiet limitation is still hiding. Try finishing this: "I grew up in France, so I speak fluent ___." You said french instantly, and the clue that told you sits all the way back at the very start of the sentence. A fixed n-gram window only sees the last word or two, so by the time it reaches the blank, "France" is long gone. Recurrent neural networks tried a gentler idea: read one token at a time and carry a small learned note forward as you go. That running note has a name worth remembering - the hidden state.

The catch is training that memory. A learning signal from far back has to travel through many steps, and at every step it gets multiplied again. That repeated multiplying is brutal: multiply by 0.9 twenty times and you fade to about 0.12; multiply by 1.1 twenty times and you balloon to roughly 6.7. So the signal tends to either vanish to nothing or explode - the famous vanishing and exploding gradient problem. Move the sliders below and watch it happen for yourself; then we will see how LSTMs and GRUs added gates to tame the flow.

One detail we have quietly glossed over: every model in this chapter still split text on spaces, exactly the way Chapter 1 did. Real models do not. They learn subword tokens, and that single choice shapes cost, speed, memory, and even fairness. It is small in code and large in consequence, so it gets its own chapter next.
