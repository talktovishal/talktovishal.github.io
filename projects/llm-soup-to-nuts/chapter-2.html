Learning by doing

Why counting words is not sufficient?

In Chapter 1 we built a real little language model out of nothing but counts. It worked! But it was also a strict librarian: if it had never seen the exact phrase before, it mostly just shrugged.

Here is the moment that nagged at me. Suppose our text has seen the cat slept but never the dog slept. You and I read that and instantly think, "well, of course a dog can sleep too" - cats and dogs turn up in the same kinds of places. But our count table keeps cat and dog in completely separate boxes, so every bit of evidence we gathered for one is useless to the other. Remember our motto from Chapter 1, you are known by the company you keep? Wouldn't it be lovely if the model knew that too?

So this chapter chases exactly that wish. We still ask the same down-to-earth question from Chapter 1 - what word might come next? - but we hand the model a new superpower: a sense that some words are alike. First we will watch the old count table fail. Then we will build a new kind of evidence, slowly and by hand, out of the company each word keeps, and then train it live in your browser. By the end, even remembering a clue from far away in a sentence will feel less mysterious.

Guiding question: can a model lean on evidence from similar words, not just identical word windows? That one idea is the bridge from the n-grams of Chapter 1 to the neural language models that power modern LLMs.

Before we dive in, a few prerequisites are in order. This chapter builds directly on Chapter 1, and every new idea is introduced slowly the moment we need it. Still, a little comfort with the following will make the ride smoother. Please use the links as quick refreshers if anything feels new.

  • Chapter 1. We reuse tokens, counts, and the question P(next | context). If "what word comes next" already feels natural, you are ready.
  • A list of numbers (a vector). The whole chapter rests on giving each word a short row of numbers, like [0.42, -0.10, 0.31]. Why this list of numbers? Because it allows us to capture the essence of a word in multiple dimensions. To understand this, think of each number as a different aspect of the word's meaning. The first number might represent the word's sentiment, the second its grammatical role, and the third its semantic similarity to other words. This is not exactly what happens in real-world applications, but it will help us visualize the concept.
  • Multiplying and adding. Our one workhorse is the dot product: multiply two lists position by position, then add the results. The geometric interpretation of the dot product is the projection of one vector onto another. This means that the dot product measures the similarity between two vectors. Thus, if two vectors are pointing in the same direction, their dot product will be positive; if they are pointing in opposite directions, the dot product will be negative; and if they are perpendicular, the dot product will be zero.

What you do not need yet: no calculus, no matrix algebra, and no neural-network experience. Those arrive in Chapter 3, right when we put them to work.

1. Limits of n-grams

The count table breaks when exact evidence disappears.

Let's start by feeling the problem for ourselves, because it is too easy to wave away in words. The n-gram from Chapter 1 was a wonderful first model: count the word windows, then lean on those counts to guess what comes next. But it is a very literal creature. If it has never seen the exact context, it cannot borrow a hunch from a similar context it has seen - even when, to us, the two are obviously cousins.

Try the small lab below. Type one two-word phrase per line. The model first hunts for that exact two-word phrase. If it cannot find it, it does the only thing it knows: it drops the first word and falls back to asking what usually followed just the last word. Watch how often the precise phrase simply is not there.

Start simple. Compare brave queen and small rabbit. Neither exact two-word phrase appears in this Alice corpus, so in both cases the model shrugs and drops the first word. But look at what is left: the fallback now leans entirely on the last word, so queen points toward said while rabbit points toward hole. The very adjective we cared about - brave, small - was thrown away completely. Useful, but blunt.

2. Neural probabilistic language models

Elevate each word to be represented as a row of numbers.

The section above let us feel the problem: a count table only trusts exact phrases. If it has seen the cat slept but not the dog slept, it has no built-in reason to let cat lend a hand to dog. The fix was first introduced by neural language models. The main idea is to stop treating words as unique identifiers, and give each one a learned representation instead.

"Representation" can sound fancy, so let's simplify it. A word vector is just a list of numbers that stands in for a word - that is the whole idea. At the very start those numbers are basically random. Then training gently nudges them, over and over, and here is the magic: words that keep showing up in similar company get nudged toward similar numbers. The company a word keeps slowly becomes the numbers a word carries.

Our browser demo uses 36 numbers per word. This is called as dimensions. There is nothing sacred about 36 - it is a knob we pick before training, the kind of choice we call a hyperparameter. Real systems often use hundreds of dimensions. We choose 36 so the training still finishes in a few seconds in your browser, yet it is rich enough to show real neighborhood patterns. Do you already feel the tradeoff :-)? More numbers can capture more nuance, but cost more to train and store.

So the plan is set: every word becomes a row of numbers. At the start those numbers are random. That raises the obvious question - nudged how, exactly? Random numbers do not improve on their own. We need a training algorithm: a repeatable little game whose rules decide, for each word, which numbers should rise and which should fall. Several such algorithms exist - the Word2Vec family, GloVe, and others - differing mostly in the game they play. We will learn the most intuitive of them, skip-gram from the Word2Vec family, because once you can picture its game the rest feel like variations. So let's put the two pieces side by side: the row of numbers we are shaping, and the game that shapes it.

A vector is a row of learned numbers

The three rows below are just a sketch to fix the picture in your mind - the browser will learn its own numbers from your text. Do not read meaning into any single value; the useful part is simply the shape: one word, one row, a handful of small numbers.

dog [0.42, -0.10, 0.31, 0.08, ...]
cat [0.39, -0.12, 0.29, 0.11, ...]
table [-0.05, 0.33, -0.18, 0.22, ...]

If dog and cat appear near many of the same words, training can make their rows more alike. The model is not given labels like "animal." It learns from word neighborhoods.

How skip-gram works?

Skip-gram plays a simple guessing game. It picks a center word and asks: which words really showed up nearby? Those genuine pairs are the ones to reward. Then negative sampling tosses in a few made-up pairs that never occurred, so the model also learns what does not belong together. Pull real pairs close, push fake pairs apart - that is the whole trick.

alice heard the rabbit nearby
center word rabbit
real nearby pairs rabbit -> the rabbit -> nearby raise the score
made-up pairs rabbit -> king rabbit -> table lower the score

This is a simplified version of the Word2Vec skip-gram idea. The original Word2Vec paper also contrasts skip-gram with CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words), which predicts a center word from its surrounding words.

What one training step does?

Quick framing first: skip-gram sets the rules of the game - which pairs count as real and which are made up. Now a training step is a single move inside that game: the tiny update the model makes to the numbers for one pair. Every word starts life as a list of random numbers, and training is just the patient work of making those numbers less random, one move at a time. Here is a single step: the model grabs a center word such as rabbit and one word from its nearby window, such as watch, and checks how well their current numbers agree.

  1. Look up the current vector for rabbit.
  2. Look up the current vector for watch.
  3. Take a dot product, remember what we discussed before about dot products helping measure similarity. This asks, "Do these two rows point in a similar direction?"
  4. Turn that score into a probability between 0 and 1 with sigmoid, an S-shaped squashing function. Why do we use sigmoid here? The sigmoid function maps any real number to a value between 0 and 1, which is perfect for representing probabilities.
  5. Compare that probability with the truth. For a real pair we wanted it high (close to 1); for a made-up pair we wanted it low (close to 0). The gap between what we got and what we wanted is the error - the only feedback the model gets.
  6. Nudge both rows to shrink that gap. For a real pair this rotates rabbit a little toward watch (and watch back toward rabbit), so their dot product - their agreement - is a touch higher next time. A made-up pair runs the exact same machinery in reverse, rotating the two rows apart. The exact arithmetic of this nudge - getting the probability, then changing the vectors - is spelled out in the box right after this list.

One nudge is tiny. Thousands of nudges happen in a single training session. One full sweep through all of those word pairs is called an epoch, and the training-depth control simply sets how many sweeps to run. That is why the loss trace matters: it shows the repeated nudges are making the training task easier for the model, and it is the same loss you will watch fall in the live Training trace below.

The nudge, written out in math

the actual skip-gram update

Step 6 said the model "nudges both rows." Here is that nudge with nothing hidden - the real skip-gram-with-negative-sampling update, and it is shorter than it looks. Write the center word's vector as v, the context word's vector as u, and the right answer as y (y = 1 for a real pair, y = 0 for a made-up one). One step is exactly these five lines - the first three you already met as steps 3 to 5, and the last two are the actual update:

s = v · u p = sigmoid(s) e = p − y v ← v − η · e · u u ← u − η · e · v

s is the score (a dot product), p is the probability the pair is real, e is the error, and η ("eta") is the learning rate - a small step size (0.035 in the worked example below). Both updates use the vectors as they were at the start of the step.

But why step along the other word's vector at all? Because the score is the dot product v · u, and a dot product grows fastest when two arrows line up. So the most effective way to make v agree more with u is simply to tip v a pinch in u's direction (and to disagree, tip it the opposite way). The partner's vector is the direction of fastest agreement - that is the whole reason u shows up in v's update, and v in u's. (In Chapter 3 you will see this "step along the partner" rule is exactly the gradient of the score.)

And why nudge both rows? Because the score treats them as equal partners - v · u is the very same number read either way - so the repair is shared: v leans toward u while u leans toward v, and the two meet in the middle twice as fast. Think of two friends spotting each other across a room and both walking forward, rather than one standing still.

Now notice how much rides on that one error number, e = p − y:

  • Real pair (y = 1). e = p − 1 is negative, so − η · e · u adds a slice of u onto v: the center word slides toward its neighbor (and the neighbor toward it). They pull together. The more the model had underrated the pair (the smaller p), the bigger the step.
  • Made-up pair (y = 0). e = p is positive, so − η · e · u subtracts a slice of u from v: the two vectors slide apart.
  • Already correct (p ≈ y). e ≈ 0, so the vectors barely move. That is why training settles down - confident-correct pairs stop tugging, and only the still-wrong pairs keep nudging.

One honest simplification: real Word2Vec keeps two vectors per word (one for its center role, one for its context role) and updates one against the other. We use a single vector per word here to match the rest of the chapter; the arithmetic is otherwise exactly the five lines above. To watch these on real numbers, see the worked example just below.

So why does this pull look-alike words together?

the key intuition

This is the part that feels like magic the first time, so let's slow down on it. Look back at the step list and notice something easy to miss: a single step only ever nudges a center word toward (or away from) the context words it actually sits beside. rabbit gets pulled toward watch, ran, hole - the company it keeps. The game never directly compares one center word with another; it never once looks at the pair rabbit → kitten.

So picture a second word that happens to keep the same company - say kitten also turns up near watch, ran, and hole. Training drags kitten toward that same set of anchor words. And here is the quiet payoff: two ropes tied to the same posts end up in the same spot. Because rabbit and kitten are both pulled toward the same handful of context vectors, they drift into the same neighborhood - close to each other - without the model ever having compared them. Their similarity is a side effect of sharing anchors.

That is the whole reason the game works. We never hand-label which words are alike; "alike" emerges because words used in the same way are tugged toward the same places. And the made-up negative pairs are the counterweight that keeps the space honest: they shove unrelated words apart, so vectors spread out into a real map instead of every word collapsing into one crowded point.

Picture the dot product as a dial of agreement. Real pairs turn each other's dial up; made-up pairs turn it down. Run that on thousands of pairs and the words that share company quietly rise to agree with the same neighbors - which is exactly what "similar vectors" means.

3. Word embeddings

Now inspect the word vectors you trained.

That trained list of numbers for a word has a name: a word embedding. In this chapter rabbit gets one embedding, queen gets one, and so on. Ours are static embeddings - the same word carries the same row of numbers everywhere it appears. (Hold on to that word "static"; in the Transformer chapter we will meet embeddings that change with context, and that contrast is the whole point.)

The browser trains vectors for up to 260 focus words from your text, and it deliberately keeps the interesting ones: characters, names, places, and other content words. It quietly drops most of the high-frequency glue - the, of, and - because a map crowded with those would teach us nothing about meaning.

Two famous methods popularized this idea. Word2Vec, which is the recipe we train here, learns from local prediction, while GloVe learns from a big table of how often words co-occur. Different recipes, same practical payoff: words become learned numbers, and nearby vectors tend to reflect related usage. Notice I said usage, not meaning. In the Alice corpus, queen may sit near other characters or shouted-dialogue words simply because that is how the book uses her - not because of any dictionary definition.

Read the map gently

The model has 36 numbers per word, but the screen has only two axes. PCA is a drawing method that squeezes the 36-number vectors into an x and y position. It is useful for a first look, but it can hide some distances and exaggerate others.

Trust neighbors more than the picture

The neighbor list compares the original 36-number vectors with cosine similarity. That means it asks whether two rows point in a similar direction. It uses the full trained vectors, not the flattened map view.

Try analogies, but stay skeptical

Classic examples like king - man + woman came from much larger training runs. Our browser model is intentionally small. Vector math can still be interesting, but failed or strange answers are part of the lesson.

Read this like evidence, not magic. If a corpus uses two words in similar places, they may become neighbors. If the corpus is small, genre-specific, or biased, the vectors can be odd or biased too. Use the "Why these neighbors?" box to inspect the context clues behind the list.

What "good" looks like here. On a single short book, expect clean neighbors mainly for words that show up often and in distinctive places: character names, pronouns, and courtesy titles like mr and miss. Rare words will look noisy, and a textbook analogy such as king − man + woman may simply miss. That is not a failure of the page; it is what a small corpus can and cannot support. For sharper neighbors, train on more text or pick a corpus where your target words are common.

4. Sequence models before Transformers

What if the useful clue is far away?

Embeddings fixed one problem - similar words can finally help each other - but a quiet limitation is still hiding. Try finishing this: "I grew up in France, so I speak fluent ___." You said french instantly, and the clue that told you sits all the way back at the very start of the sentence. A fixed n-gram window only sees the last word or two, so by the time it reaches the blank, "France" is long gone. Recurrent neural networks tried a gentler idea: read one token at a time and carry a small learned note forward as you go. That running note has a name worth remembering - the hidden state.

The catch is training that memory. A learning signal from far back has to travel through many steps, and at every step it gets multiplied again. That repeated multiplying is brutal: multiply by 0.9 twenty times and you fade to about 0.12; multiply by 1.1 twenty times and you balloon to roughly 6.7. So the signal tends to either vanish to nothing or explode - the famous vanishing and exploding gradient problem. Move the sliders below and watch it happen for yourself; then we will see how LSTMs and GRUs added gates to tame the flow.

One detail we have quietly glossed over: every model in this chapter still split text on spaces, exactly the way Chapter 1 did. Real models do not. They learn subword tokens, and that single choice shapes cost, speed, memory, and even fairness. It is small in code and large in consequence, so it gets its own chapter next.
