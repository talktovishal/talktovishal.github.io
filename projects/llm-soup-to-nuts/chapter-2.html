Learning by doing

When Count Tables Need Help.

In Chapter 1, our model remembered exact word windows. That was useful. It was also strict. If the exact window was missing, the model had very little to say.

Suppose the text has seen the cat slept, but not the dog slept. A count table treats those as two unrelated cases. You and I see the connection right away: cats and dogs tend to show up in the same kinds of places. What we want is for the model to learn a little of that connection too.

This chapter is about that next step. We still ask the same practical question: what word might come next? First we will watch exact counts fail. Then we will build a new kind of evidence, slowly, from the words around each word. After that, longer memory and better token choices will feel less mysterious.

Guiding question: can a model use evidence from similar words, not just identical word windows? That is the bridge from n-grams to neural language models.

7. Limits of n-grams

The count table breaks when exact evidence disappears.

N-grams are a great first model: they count word windows, then lean on those counts to guess the next word. But the rule is very literal. If the exact context is missing, the model has no real way to borrow help from a similar context it has already seen.

Try the small lab below. Type one two-word phrase per line. The model first looks for that exact two-word phrase. If it cannot find it, the table shows a simpler fallback: it keeps only the last word and asks what usually came after that word.

Start simple. Compare the dog and the man. The exact two-word phrases may both be missing. But the fallback words are different: dog and man. That is why the fallback guesses can differ.

8. Neural probabilistic language models

First, give each word a small row of numbers.

Section 7 showed the problem. A count table only trusts exact phrases. If it has seen the cat slept but not the dog slept, it has no built-in reason to let cat help dog. Neural language models changed that by giving each word a learned representation.

A representation can sound fancy, so let's make it plain. A word vector is just a short list of numbers for one word. At the start, those numbers are almost random. During training, the model nudges them. Words that show up in similar neighborhoods get nudged toward similar numbers.

This browser demo now uses 36 numbers per word. That number is not a law of nature. It is a choice we make before training, called a hyperparameter. Larger systems often use many more numbers. We use 36 so the training is still browser-sized, but rich enough to show real neighborhood patterns.

Learn more: Bengio et al. on distributed word representations, the first Word2Vec paper, and negative sampling in Word2Vec.

A vector is a row of learned numbers

The numbers below are only a sketch. The browser learns its own numbers from the selected text. The useful part is the shape: one word, one row, many small values.

dog [0.42, -0.10, 0.31, 0.08, ...]
cat [0.39, -0.12, 0.29, 0.11, ...]
table [-0.05, 0.33, -0.18, 0.22, ...]

If dog and cat appear near many of the same words, training can make their rows more alike. The model is not given labels like "animal." It learns from word neighborhoods.

What skip-gram trains on

Skip-gram starts with a center word. It asks: which nearby words really appeared around this center word? Then negative sampling adds a few made-up pairs, so the model also learns what does not fit.

alice heard the rabbit nearby
center word rabbit
real nearby pairs rabbit -> the rabbit -> nearby raise the score
made-up pairs rabbit -> king rabbit -> table lower the score

This is a simplified redraw of the Word2Vec skip-gram idea. The original Word2Vec paper also contrasts skip-gram with CBOW, which predicts a center word from its surrounding words.

What one training step does

Each word starts with a small list of random numbers. Training is the repeated work of making those numbers less random. In one step, the model takes a center word such as rabbit and one word from its nearby window, such as watch.

  1. Look up the current vector for rabbit.
  2. Look up the current vector for watch.
  3. Take a dot product. This asks, "Do these two rows point in a similar direction?"
  4. Turn that score into a probability between 0 and 1 with sigmoid, an S-shaped squashing function.
  5. Compare the probability with the answer: real pair or made-up pair.
  6. Nudge the numbers so the next score is a little less wrong.

One nudge is tiny. Thousands of nudges create the pattern. One full sweep through all of those word pairs is called an epoch, and the training-depth control simply sets how many sweeps to run. That is why the loss trace matters: it shows the repeated nudges are making the training task easier for the model.

score = center vector · context vector probability real = sigmoid(score) loss = surprise about the right answer

Why fake pairs are useful

If rabbit → nearby really appeared, training nudges the vectors so that score goes up. If rabbit → table was a made-up negative example, training nudges the score down. After many passes, words with similar neighborhoods receive similar nudges, so their final lists of numbers become more alike.

This is why nearby words mean similar usage in this corpus. They are not guaranteed to be synonyms.

9. Word embeddings

Now inspect the word vectors you trained.

A word embedding is the trained vector for one word. In this chapter, rabbit gets one vector, queen gets one vector, and so on. These are static embeddings: the same word has the same row of numbers wherever it appears in this small model.

The browser trains vectors for up to 260 focus words from your selected text. The focus vocabulary keeps characters, names, places, and other content words. It removes most high-frequency glue words from the map, because a cloud of the, of, and and is not the lesson we want here.

Word2Vec learns from local prediction. GloVe learns from a larger co-occurrence table. Both helped popularize the same practical idea: words can be represented by learned numbers, and nearby vectors often reflect related usage. This is not the same as a dictionary definition. In the Alice corpus, queen may sit near other characters, court words, or shouted-dialogue words because those are the situations where the book uses her.

Learn more: Word2Vec CBOW and skip-gram, negative sampling and analogies, GloVe, and PCA documentation.

Read the map gently

The model has 36 numbers per word, but the screen has only two axes. PCA is a drawing method that squeezes the 36-number vectors into an x and y position. It is useful for a first look, but it can hide some distances and exaggerate others.

Trust neighbors more than the picture

The neighbor list compares the original 36-number vectors with cosine similarity. That means it asks whether two rows point in a similar direction. It uses the full trained vectors, not the flattened map view.

Try analogies, but stay skeptical

Classic examples like king - man + woman came from much larger training runs. Our browser model is intentionally small. Vector math can still be interesting, but failed or strange answers are part of the lesson.

Read this like evidence, not magic. If a corpus uses two words in similar places, they may become neighbors. If the corpus is small, genre-specific, or biased, the vectors can be odd or biased too. Use the "Why these neighbors?" box to inspect the context clues behind the list.

10. Sequence models before Transformers

What if the useful clue is far away?

Think of finishing a sentence like "I grew up in France, so I speak fluent ___." The word that fits is french, but the clue that points to it sits all the way back at the start. A fixed n-gram window only sees the last word or two, so by the time we reach the blank that evidence is long gone. Recurrent neural networks tried a gentler idea: read one token at a time, and carry a small learned note forward as you go. That running note is called a hidden state.

The hard part is training that memory. A learning signal from far back in the sentence has to travel through many steps, and at each step it gets multiplied again. That repeated multiplying is unforgiving: a factor of 0.9 applied twenty times shrinks to about 0.12, while a factor of 1.1 applied twenty times grows to roughly 6.7. So the signal tends to either fade to almost nothing or blow up. LSTMs and GRUs added gates to tame that flow.

11. Why tokenization matters

Tokenization comes back, but now the stakes are higher.

Chapter 1 used a simple tokenizer so we could count words. Here the tokenizer becomes part of the model design. The model does not read raw text. It reads token IDs. So the boundary choices affect cost, speed, and what patterns the model can learn.

Whole words are easy to read, but rare words break them. Characters and bytes can represent almost anything, but they make longer sequences. Subwords are the middle path. They keep common pieces whole and split rarer words into useful chunks.

What is new here? In Chapter 1, tokenization helped us make a count table. Here, tokenization decides how much work the neural model does and how unfamiliar words are represented.

12. Tokenizer algorithms

Now let the tokenizer learn its pieces.

A hand-written splitter is easy, but modern tokenizers are usually trained. They look at a corpus and decide which pieces are worth keeping. That is why common chunks stay short and rare words can still be spelled with smaller pieces.

This lab is small, but real. BPE merges frequent neighboring pieces. WordPiece-style encoding picks the longest known piece it can. Unigram-style encoding chooses a likely segmentation from learned pieces. Byte-level encoding uses the actual UTF-8 bytes.

13. Tokenization edge cases

Always inspect what the model actually receives.

Edge cases are not trivia. They are where hidden assumptions show up. Multilingual text, code, whitespace, numbers, emoji, rare names, and domain vocabulary can all change the token stream.

Pick a case and inspect the output. Sometimes the visible text and the tokenized input are not as similar as they look. That can affect cost, evidence, safety, and fairness.
