Learning by doing

The math that makes a model guess the next word.

Chapters 1 and 2 kept asking one question: what word comes next? This chapter picks up the same question and answers it a new way. Instead of counting word windows, we will build a tiny neural network that learns to predict the next word, and we will train it right here in the browser.

To get there we need a small toolkit of math. The good news is that every tool earns its place by solving a problem you can see. Probability measures how surprised the model is by the real next word. Vectors let it compare words. Loss turns a wrong guess into a single number. Gradients say which way to nudge the numbers so the next guess is a little better.

We keep the example tiny on purpose, because a tiny model lets you watch the whole loop: words become numbers, numbers become next-word probabilities, a loss is measured, and the parameters move a little. A modern LLM is a much, much larger version of this exact loop.

Guiding question: how can a pile of numbers learn to finish your sentence, when nobody wrote the grammar rules by hand?

Compare with vectors Give each word numbers, then score candidates with dot products and similarity.

1. Surprise and the next word

Start where Chapter 1 left off: how surprised is the model?

Picture the tiny corpus we will train on at the end of this chapter. Across its sentences, the words the cat are followed by sat 8 times, ran 3 times, and slept once. That is 12 examples, so the conditional probability of sat coming next is P(sat | the cat) = 8 / 12 ≈ 0.67. The vertical bar means "given." We are not asking how common sat is everywhere, only how common it is right after the cat.

A good model should put high probability on the words that really do come next. Cross-entropy measures how surprised the model is by the real next word, in bits. Entropy is the smallest surprise possible, the floor you would hit if your guesses matched the evidence exactly. Perplexity rewrites that surprise on a friendlier scale: roughly how many words the model felt torn between.

Play with the controls. The left sliders are the evidence (how often each word followed the context). The right sliders are the model's guessed probabilities. Watch cross-entropy fall as the guesses move toward the evidence. That is exactly what training will do for us automatically.

Cross-entropy by hand

With the evidence sat 8, ran 3, slept 1 and a model that guesses sat 55%, ran 30%, slept 15%, the surprise at each real word is -log2(model probability), weighted by how often that word actually happened:

P(sat) = 8/12 = 0.67, P(ran) = 3/12 = 0.25, P(slept) = 1/12 = 0.08 cross-entropy = 0.67·(-log2 0.55) + 0.25·(-log2 0.30) + 0.08·(-log2 0.15) ≈ 0.58 + 0.43 + 0.23 = 1.24 bits → perplexity = 2^1.24 ≈ 2.4 words

The live panel recomputes these as you drag. A perfect model would reach the entropy floor of about 1.19 bits; the gap above it is the model's avoidable surprise, and training exists to shrink it.

Probability How much chance the model gives one next word, from 0 to 1.
Conditional probability The chance of a word given the context, like sat given the cat.
Entropy The unavoidable surprise when the context truly allows several next words.
Cross-entropy The model's real surprise at the actual next word. Training drives this down.
Perplexity Cross-entropy as a count: about how many next words the model is torn between.

2. Words as vectors

Give each word a few numbers, and comparison becomes geometry.

Chapter 2 gave every word a short list of learned numbers, its vector. Stack all those rows together and you have an embedding matrix: one row per word. Picking a word is just choosing its row. Here we shrink each word to two numbers so we can draw it as an arrow.

Why bother? Because the model scores the next word with a dot product. Multiply the matching components of two vectors and add them up. The dot product is large when two arrows point the same way, near zero when they are unrelated, and negative when they point apart. Cosine similarity is the same idea using direction only; distance asks how far apart the tips are. That single number, the dot product, is how the model will rank every candidate word later.

What the comparison says

dot product

When the model ranks the next word, it compares a context vector against each candidate word vector with exactly this dot product. Bigger score, higher rank. Attention, much later, runs the same comparison at a far larger scale.

One dot product, by hand

Say cat = [0.7, 0.3] and dog = [0.6, 0.4]. Their similarity score is just multiply-and-add:

dot(cat, dog) = 0.7×0.6 + 0.3×0.4 = 0.42 + 0.12 = 0.54 cosine = dot ÷ (length cat × length dog) ≈ 0.54 ÷ (0.76 × 0.72) ≈ 0.98

A cosine near 1 means cat and dog are used in almost the same direction, so the model treats them as interchangeable context. That shared strength is the whole reason Chapter 2 moved from exact counts to learned vectors.

3. Gradients, gradient descent, and loss

When the guess is wrong, which way do we nudge?

So far we can measure surprise and score words. Training is the part that actually reduces the surprise. To see it clearly, we strip the model down to the simplest thing that can learn: a straight line with two knobs, slope and intercept. The loss here is mean squared error, the average squared gap between the line and the dots. Our next-word model will swap in cross-entropy loss instead, but the nudging idea is identical.

The gradient is the slope of the loss with respect to each knob: which way is downhill. If a knob's gradient is positive, nudging that knob down lowers the loss. Gradient descent just repeats that nudge, a small step size at a time. Press Step descent and watch the line walk toward the dots.

Loss and gradients

mean squared error

One step, by hand

Take a single point (x = 1, y = 1) and a line currently set to slope = 0, intercept = 0, so it predicts 0. The squared-error loss and its gradients are:

error = prediction − y = 0 − 1 = −1, loss = error² = 1 gradient on slope = 2·error·x = 2·(−1)·1 = −2 new slope = slope − stepSize·gradient = 0 − 0.1·(−2) = +0.2

A negative gradient means "go up," so the update nudges the slope upward toward the point. Repeat across all points and the line settles into the cloud. Every weight in the next-word model is trained by this same subtraction, just thousands of them at once.

4. Softmax and assigning blame

Turn raw word scores into next-word probabilities.

Our network will end with one raw score, called a logit, for every word it could predict. Scores are not probabilities, so softmax fixes that: it exponentiates each score (making it positive), then divides by the total so the numbers add up to 1. Temperature stretches or sharpens the result, the same knob you met when generating text in Chapter 1.

Once we have probabilities, cross-entropy asks how much the model put on the word that actually came next. Backpropagation is just careful bookkeeping for blame: for the predicted word it says "too high, push down," and for the true word "too low, push up." For softmax with cross-entropy that blame is beautifully simple: probability − target.

5. Build the forward pass

Stack the pieces into a tiny next-word network.

Now we assemble the toolkit. A neuron multiplies its inputs by weights, adds a bias, and passes the result through an activation function. Activations such as ReLU, tanh, and sigmoid add bends and curves so a stack of layers can learn more than a straight line. Several neurons side by side make a layer; stacked layers make a multilayer perceptron, or MLP.

Here is the whole forward pass for predicting the next word: look up an embedding row for each context word, join them, push them through a hidden layer with an activation, and finish with softmax over the candidate words. Change the context or the activation and watch the prediction move. (These weights are fixed and illustrative; in the next section the model learns its own.)

Each context word is an index into the embedding table; its row is a parameter the network can tune. Read the steps top to bottom: two lookups, one activated hidden layer, raw logits, then softmax probabilities.

This is exactly the shape of the model you are about to train: context words in, a next-word distribution out. The only thing missing is learning, which is the four-beat loop of forward pass, loss, backward pass, and update.

7. Train a next-word model

Now train the real thing: a tiny neural language model.

This is the payoff. Everything above becomes one small network that predicts the next word, trained right here on a tiny corpus. It is a miniature of the neural language model Bengio and colleagues introduced in 2003: each context word is looked up in an embedding table, the rows are joined and pushed through a hidden layer with a tanh activation, and softmax produces a probability for every word in the vocabulary. Cross-entropy scores each guess and backprop nudges every weight, including the embedding rows themselves.

Press Train and watch three things move together: the loss falls, perplexity drops from "torn between many words" toward "a confident handful," and the prediction bars for a context like the cat sharpen onto the words that really follow it. Then generate a sentence and see the same math from Chapter 1, sampling one word at a time, but now driven by learned vectors instead of raw counts.

Take a moment to appreciate what just happened. With nothing but probability, vectors, a dot product, softmax, and a gradient nudge, a pile of numbers taught itself to finish sentences. That is the whole engine of a modern LLM, only tiny. It still has one real limit, though: it reads a fixed two-word window and learns every context on its own, much like the n-grams back in Chapter 1. The rest of the course lifts that limit. Next we pin down how raw text becomes the tokens a model actually reads, and soon after we meet the Transformer, the architecture that finally lets a model look across a whole sentence at once.
