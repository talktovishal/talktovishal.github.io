Learning by doing

Language Models before ChatGPT (LLMs) :-).

Language modeling from first principles: let's build a tiny next-word predictor.

I type a few words on my phone, and before I finish the sentence, the keyboard suggests what might come next. How does that happen? Let's build one by ourselves!

We will start from text, turn it into pieces - words or tokens. We keep a tally of how many times a word occurred and also it's neigbors. Why? Remember how you are known by the company you keep, the same is true for words! We then ask a practical question over and over: when these words appeared before, what usually came next? That simple question gives us a real language model, even though it is much smaller than a modern LLM.

Guiding question: if I type Happy Birthday to you, Happy Birthday to why does you feel like a reasonable next word, but after only to the next word feels much less certain?

Before we dive in, a few needed prerequisites are in order. We build every idea slowly inside the chapter and cover the necessary concepts. However some basics are required. Please use the links as quick refreshers if any of the concepts feel new to you.

  • Probability basics. A probability is just a number from 0 to 1 (or 0–100%). We lean a lot on conditional probability ‐ the chance of the next word given the words so far, written P(next | context) ‐ and on the idea that the chances for all the options add up to 1.
  • Fractions, division, and percentages. We turn counts into probabilities by dividing, for example 41 / 303 = 13.5%.
  • Logarithms and exponents. These appear in one place, the surprise and perplexity section. Knowing roughly what log does, and that exp undoes it, is enough; we build up the rest there.
  • Reading a little code. The exercises show short snippets of JavaScript you can run and tweak. You never have to write any from scratch, but being comfortable reading a function and its return value helps.

What you do not need yet: no calculus, no vectors or linear algebra, and no prior machine-learning background. Those ideas arrive in later chapters, right at the moment we put them to use.

1. From text to tokens

Break text into smaller pieces the model can count.

If I want my phone to suggest the next word, it first needs examples of writing to learn from. It needs to understand the patterns in the text to make good predictions. It's called learning from data. In our small version, we learn can from a public-domain book, a Wikipedia-style article, or a few paragraphs we write ourselves. That collection of text used for training the model is called a corpus.

Remember how we take small bites of the food? In the same way, we need to break down the text into smaller pieces that the model can process. Humans can casually say, "split it into words," but the computer needs a rule it can apply again. Humans call the smaller units words and in computer science lingo we call the smaller units tokens. In this lesson a token can be a word like alice, a word like rabbit, or even a punctuation mark like ,. To re-iterate, a word is the thing we talk about in ordinary language; a token is the exact piece we hand to the computer program aka the model.

Why is tokenization not straightforward?
Should don't stay as one token, become do plus n't, or be treated like do not? And in languages such as Chinese or Thai, spaces do not mark words the way they usually do in English. So even this first step, deciding where one piece ends and the next begins, can become a surprisingly complicated problem.

After tokenization, I want you to notice two different views of the same text. The token stream keeps the full order, including repeats, because counts depend on how often things happened. The vocabulary keeps the unique tokens. If the appears 900 times, the stream keeps all 900 appearances in order, but the vocabulary contains the once. Vocabulary is the menu of tokens (words for human language) this simple model can ever predict or guess.

Corpus

Think of the corpus as the model's reading pile. This simple model cannot invent evidence from outside that pile, so a fairy-tale corpus, a news corpus, and a technical corpus will all lead to different predictions.

A token is the exact piece we let the computer count. In this page, alice, rabbit, and , can all be tokens. We can make a choice to lowercase so that Alice and alice should become the same piece. Think about why would this be useful and if it's always a good idea :-)? Can you think of words that have the same spelling but different meanings depending on the context and the casing? Consider a word like "Big" what if someone's name is "Big"? Would you then like to treat "Big" and "big" as the same token? What if "Big" is a proper noun and "big" is an adjective? How would you handle that? We will deal with these questions as we explore the world of tokens.

2. n-gram - How much context does the model need?

Give the model memory and tell it how much to remember.

Now that the corpus has become tokens, we can ask the question that makes prediction interesting: how much of the recent past should we look at? If I only say fox, the next word could go in many directions. If I say the quick brown fox, many of us immediately expect something like jumps. The extra words narrow the guess.

Try this intuition: after the, almost any noun could come next. After said the, a story corpus might point toward king, queen, hatter, or another character. We are not changing the model's memory here; we are changing which evidence or context it is allowed to use. Remember this word context, we will encounter it again when we learn about ChatGPT. Do you see that why more context can be better, but also more complex or expensive since you have to process more information?

Now let's introduce the concept of n-gram. It just means a window of n tokens. A unigram is a one-token window, so it only knows what tokens are common overall. A bigram is a two-token window, so it can remember one previous token. A trigram is a three-token window, so it can remember two previous tokens. In probability terms, the trigram model calculates the probability of a word conditioned on the two previous words.

So when the context is said the, a trigram asks, "When this exact two-token context appeared, what followed?" A bigram ignores said and only asks what followed the. A unigram ignores the local context and asks what tokens are common in general. The tradeoff is the heart of this section: more memory can be more precise, but it also creates more exact contexts the model may never have seen. This is an important concept in natural language processing (NLP). More context can be better, but it also means that you are more specialized in the types of patterns you can recognize. Hence, we will see later how to manage when you see context which you have not seen before. How can you still make the predictions? Say I have seen the cat sat on the mat, and now I see the phrase the dog sat on the .... As a human, we will guess the next token to be mat since we know that cat and dog are both animals that can sit on a mat. However, our current model doesn't know this. We will see later how to make our model more robust in handling such cases.

There is a quiet assumption hiding in all of this, and it even has a name: the Markov assumption. What it means is that the probability of the next word depends only on the current word (or a fixed number of previous words). That is clearly not the whole truth, since real sentences lean on far more than two words. But it is a wonderfully useful simplification, because it turns the impossible job of remembering everything into the manageable job of counting short windows.

3. Calculating probabilities from counts

Turn repeated patterns into next-token probabilities.

At this point, we have counted small windows aka contexts inside the corpus. For each context, the model can ask a plain question: "When I saw this before, what toekn came next?" If one token occurred many times and another happened once, we want the model to treat the first token as more likely.

The move from counts to probability is division. We count how often a next token appeared after a context, then divide by the total number of times that context appeared: P(next | context) = count(context, next) / count(context). The vertical bar means "given." or "conditioned on." So I read P(king | said the) as "the probability of king given that the current context is said the."

Example: if king followed said the 41 times, and said the appeared 303 times total, then P(king | said the) = 41 / 303 = 13.5%. We are calculating the share of the evidence points to this next token.

Here is a small but reassuring detail. If you add up these shares across every token that ever followed said the, they come to exactly 1, which is just another way of writing 100%. That is what makes this a real probability distribution: the context has one whole unit of confidence to give away, and it only has to decide how much of it each possible next token deserves.

The important detail is that this probability is local. It does not mean king is always a likely English word. It means king was common after this context in this corpus. Change the corpus, change the context, or change the tokenizer, and the probability table can change. That is why the visualization shows a distribution, not just one winning word.

4. Prediction leads to generation

Print one token at a time.

Once the model can predict one next token, we can ask it to keep going. This is the moment our little model starts to feel like the phone keyboard from the opening: we hand it a seed phrase, let it choose a next token, attach that token to the end, and then ask again from the new ending. In a trigram model, the text is built from many tiny choices, and each choice only looks back at the last two tokens.

The probability table tells us what could come next, but it does not by itself decide which token we should actually choose. One simple rule is top-pick: always take the token with the largest probability. That is stable and easy to inspect, but it can become repetitive. Another rule is sampling: choose randomly, but weigh the random choice by the probabilities.

Think of it like a weighted draw. If one token has 70% probability and another has 10%, top-pick always chooses the 70% token. Sampling usually chooses it too, but every so often it lets the 10% token through. That is why two runs from the same prompt can either feel locked in or pleasantly varied.

This is also the bridge to modern language models. Their probabilities come from a neural network rather than from an n-gram count table, but the decoding question is still recognizable: after we have a probability for every possible next token, do we always take the most likely one, or do we allow controlled variation?

5. When exact context is missing

Back off to evidence the model has actually seen.

Here is where the extra memory from a trigram can hurt us. Suppose we ask what comes after softly said. The trigram is very specific: it wants to know what followed that exact two-token context. If the exact phrase never appeared in the corpus, the model has no trigram evidence, even though said by itself may be very common.

This is why backoff exists. I like to think of it as relaxing the question. If the detailed question has no evidence, ask a simpler question. Instead of "what followed softly said?", ask "what followed said?" If that also fails, fall back again to the unigram model i.e just predict based on the most common tokens. We lose precision, but we often gain enough evidence to keep moving.

Backoff is not magic. It does not know what softly said should mean. It simply notices that the exact trigram is missing and reuses a shorter, better-supported context. In the excercise below, watch the chain of evidence: the highlighted row is the evidence the model actually uses.

Smoothing solves a related, but slightly different, problem. Backoff helps when the context itself is missing. Smoothing helps when the context exists, but one possible next token has a count of zero. In a small training corpus, zero count is a dangerous thing to believe too strongly. It may mean "this can never happen," but it may also mean "this just did not happen in the slice of text we trained on."

Let me explain. Suppose the model is using the context said the, which appears often in the Alice corpus. The count table may say that king, hatter, and mock followed that context, while a known word such as rabbit never did. Raw counting gives P(rabbit | said the) = 0. That zero is brittle: generation can never choose rabbit there, and evaluation becomes infinitely surprised if a held-out sentence actually contains that next token. Smoothing is a way to say, "I mostly trust the counts, but I will leave a tiny door open for known words I did not observe in this exact spot."

Add-alpha smoothing does that by adding the same small imaginary count to every known vocabulary token before we divide: (count + alpha) / (context total + alpha * |V|). The numerator gives each token a little extra evidence. The denominator grows by the total imaginary evidence we added across the whole vocabulary. So alpha is a humility knob: a little alpha removes harsh zeros, while too much alpha flattens the model and steals probability from the words the corpus really did show often.

Watch for the tradeoff. When you move alpha from 0.0 to 0.1 or 0.5, rare or unseen known tokens become possible, but the strongest observed choices become less dominant. Smoothing is not making the model smarter; it is making the model less overconfident about what a small corpus failed to show.

Raw count: P(next | context) = count(context + next) / count(context)

Add-alpha smoothing: P(next | context) = (count + alpha) / (context total + alpha * |V|)

6. Test (Evaluate) on held-out (aka unseen) text

Measure surprise with perplexity.

Now that the model can generate a few amusing sentences, is it actually any good at prediction? One common way to test that is to keep a small piece of text\corpora separate from the training data, not train on it, and then ask the model to predict it token by token. That kept-aside text is called a holdout set.

For each token in the holdout, we look at the probability the model gave to the token that actually appeared. If the model gave the real next token high probability, it was not very surprised. If it gave that token tiny probability, it was very surprised. Perplexity is a way to summarize that surprise. Lower perplexity means the model's guesses fit the held-out text better.

The math version: if the actual next-token probabilities are p_1, p_2, ..., p_N, average surprise is H = -(1 / N) * sum(log(p_i)), and perplexity is PPL = exp(H). Good predictions make the p_i values larger, which makes the surprise smaller, which pushes perplexity down.

That formula looks dense, so let me build it up one step at a time.

Step 1, the surprise of one guess: -log(p_i). We want a surprise score that is zero when the model was sure and correct, and grows as the probability it gave the true token shrinks. The function -log(p) does exactly that. When p = 1 the model was certain, and -log(1) = 0: no surprise at all. As p slides toward zero the score climbs without limit. The neat part is that every time the probability is cut in half, the surprise goes up by the same fixed step. That matches how surprise actually feels: moving from "even odds" to "one in four" is the same jump as moving from "one in four" to "one in eight."

Each halving of probability adds one constant step of surprise (here, one bit):

p = 1 → 0    p = 1/2 → 1    p = 1/4 → 2    p = 1/8 → 3

The base of the log just sets the unit (base 2 gives bits, the natural log gives "nats"). It does not change perplexity, as long as the final exp uses the same base as the log.

Step 2, why a logarithm, and why the pieces add up? To score the whole holdout text, we need to calculate the probability the model assigns to the entire passage. By the chain rule, that is the product of every per-token probability: p_1 × p_2 × ... × p_N. Multiplying hundreds of small numbers collapses toward zero and is impossible to read. A logarithm rescues us, because it turns a product into a sum: log(p_1 × ... × p_N) = log(p_1) + ... + log(p_N). So adding up the per-token surprises is not an arbitrary trick; it is just measuring the (negative) log-probability of the whole text, one token at a time.

Step 3, why divide by N? A longer passage piles up more total surprise simply because it has more tokens, so a raw sum would punish a holdout just for being long. Dividing by the number of tokens N turns the total into an average surprise per token. That per-token rate, which we call H, is what lets us compare a short sentence against a long paragraph fairly.

Step 4, why take an exponential at the end? H is measured in log units, which are abstract on their own. Exponentiating undoes the log and lands us back on something we can actually picture: an effective count of choices. If a model spread its probability evenly over K equally likely words at every step, each p_i would be 1/K, the average surprise would be log(K), and exp(log(K)) = K exactly. So PPL = exp(H) is answering a concrete question: how many equally likely words is the model effectively choosing between?

Concrete example. Suppose the model makes two guesses and gives the true token a probability of 1/2 each time. Each guess costs -log(1/2) of surprise, so the average is H = -log(1/2) and PPL = exp(-log(1/2)) = 2: the model was as unsure as a fair coin flip between two words. A quick way to sanity-check the whole formula is this shortcut: when the model gives the true token the same probability p at every step, perplexity comes out to exactly 1/p. Give it 1/2 each time and perplexity is 2; give it 1/4 each time and perplexity is 4, which is exactly the "how many equally likely options" reading.

If that formula still feels abstract, here is the picture I keep in my head. Perplexity is roughly how many words the model feels like it is choosing between at each step. A perplexity of 30 means it is about as unsure as someone guessing blindly among 30 equally likely words, while a perplexity of 5 means it has narrowed the field to a handful. So lower perplexity simply means fewer options the model is torn between, which is one more way of saying it was less surprised.

This is also why a bigram or trigram often beats a unigram on matched text. A unigram spreads probability across words that are common anywhere in the corpus. A bigram can notice that after the, nouns are much more plausible than many other words. A trigram can sometimes narrow the guess even further: after said the, this corpus often points toward character words. When the extra context is reliable, the actual holdout token gets a larger p_i, so -log(p_i) is smaller and perplexity drops. When the extra context is too rare, the opposite can happen, which is why smoothing and backoff matter.

If you see infinite perplexity, the page is not broken. It means at least one real holdout token received probability zero, and log(0) does not behave like an ordinary number. In this small model, that usually means the context was unseen or the holdout contains a token outside the training vocabulary. Smoothing can help with known tokens, but it cannot rescue a word the model never saw as a token at all.

7. Bridge to modern LLMs

What this tiny model teaches about LLMs.

I like starting with n-grams because they are simple and intuitive. Modern neural language models, aka LLMs like the one that power ChatGPT, do not keep a simple count table like this one, but the learning loop will be familiar. They will learn from text, represent context, estimate next-token probabilities, choose from those probabilities, and test on text the model did not train on.

The big difference is how the model represents evidence. Our n-gram model remembers exact local patterns: said the and asked the live in separate count boxes even if they behave similarly. Neural LLMs learn internal representations where related words, phrases, and situations can share statistical strength. That is one reason they can generalize beyond exact repeated windows.

What carries forward: training text becomes evidence, context changes the next-token distribution, and generation is still a sequence of choices. The machinery becomes much more powerful, but the question P(next | context) never really leaves the room.

The other difference is context length. A trigram sees only two previous tokens; an LLM can condition on a much larger prompt, including instructions, examples, and earlier conversation. So the modern model is not "just an n-gram." But after this lesson, the basic shape should feel less foreign: context goes in, probabilities come out, and decoding turns those probabilities into text.

Further reading.

If you want to keep going, these are the references I would reach for: more detailed notes, textbook treatments, implementation guides, and a bit of history. Start with Jurafsky and Martin for the main narrative, Collins for a compact mathematical treatment, and NLTK or KenLM when you want to connect these ideas to working language-model code.

  2. Jurafsky and Martin, Chapter 2: Words and Tokens A deeper look at tokenization, word types, subwords, and why languages without spaces make token boundaries interesting.
  3. Jurafsky and Martin, Appendix C: Kneser-Ney Smoothing A short technical follow-up once add-alpha smoothing and backoff feel comfortable.

  8. Katz Back-off Model A useful reference for the formal backoff idea after seeing the ladder in this lesson.
  9. Kneser-Ney Smoothing A readable overview of one of the most important smoothing ideas for serious n-gram models.
  10. Perplexity The information-theory view: perplexity as exponentiated entropy or average surprise.
  11. Google Research: All Our N-gram are Belong to You A historical snapshot of what web-scale n-gram count tables look like.
  12. Google Books Ngram Viewer A quick way to explore phrase frequencies over a very large book corpus, with the usual corpus-bias caveats.
