Learning by doing

The math that teaches a model to guess the next word :-).

I keep coming back to the same tiny miracle from Chapter 1: I type a few words, and something guesses what comes next. Counting got us part of the way. Now let's make the numbers learn the guess instead of storing every little count table by hand. We will build a tiny neural network, train it start to finish right here in your browser, and watch a pile of numbers learn to finish sentences.

Yes, there is math here. But every tool earns its keep by solving a problem you can actually see. Probability puts a number on how surprised the model is by the real next word. Vectors let it compare words. Loss squeezes a wrong guess into a single number. Gradients whisper which way to nudge the numbers so the next guess is a little better. We meet each one only when the model needs it, never as a bare definition.

We keep the example deliberately tiny, because a tiny model lets you watch the whole loop: words become numbers, numbers become next-word probabilities, a loss is measured, and the parameters shuffle a little closer to right. A modern LLM is a much, much larger version of this exact loop - same four beats, just billions of numbers instead of a handful.

Guiding question: how can a pile of numbers learn to finish your sentence, when nobody wrote the grammar rules by hand? That is the mystery this whole chapter slowly takes apart.

Measure surprise Turn next-word counts into probability, entropy, cross-entropy, and perplexity.
Compare with vectors Give each word numbers, then score candidates with dot products and similarity.
Nudge with gradients Use loss, gradients, gradient descent, softmax, and backprop.
Train the real thing Assemble a small neural network and teach it to predict the next word.

Before we dive in, a few needed prerequisites are in order. This chapter leans on Chapters 1 and 2, and it builds every piece of math slowly, the moment we actually need it. A little comfort with the following will make the ride smoother - use the links as quick refreshers if anything feels new.

  • Chapter 1. We reuse next-word counts turned into probability (P(next | context)) and the idea of perplexity as how "surprised" a model is.
  • Chapter 2. We reuse the big idea that each word is a short row of numbers (a vector), and that a dot product compares two of them.
  • Multiply, add, compare. That is the entire math vocabulary on this page. If you can multiply two numbers and add the results, you can follow every formula here.

What you do not need yet: no calculus and no matrix algebra. We build gradients, softmax, and backprop up from scratch, one tiny nudge at a time - the only moves are multiply, add, and compare.

1. Surprise and the next word

Start where Chapter 1 left off: how surprised is the model?

Let's begin with a feeling you already have: surprise. Read "the cat ___" and your mind quietly bets on a couple of words - sat, maybe ran. End the sentence on sat and you feel nothing; end it on helicopter and you flinch. A language model has that exact reflex, and here is the part that surprised me the first time I saw it: all of training is just teaching the model to flinch less at the words that really come next. So our very first job is to put a number on surprise.

Here is the tiny corpus we will train on at the end of this chapter. Across its sentences, the cat is followed by sat 8 times, ran 3 times, and slept once - 12 examples in all. So the chance of sat coming next, written P(sat | the cat) = 8 / 12 ≈ 0.67, is just 8 out of those 12. That vertical bar means "given": we are not asking how common sat is everywhere, only how common it is right after the cat.

Now the three words this whole section turns on. Cross-entropy is the model's actual surprise at the real next word, counted in bits. Entropy is the smallest surprise anyone could manage on this context - the floor you reach only if your guesses match the evidence perfectly. And perplexity rewrites that surprise on a friendlier scale: roughly how many words the model felt torn between. Three names, but they all answer one question - is the model confident, or flailing?

Now feel it for yourself. The left sliders are the evidence - how often each word actually followed the context. The right sliders are the model's guesses. Drag a guess away from the evidence and watch cross-entropy climb; drag it back and watch the surprise fall toward the entropy floor. That falling number is exactly what training will do for us, automatically, thousands of times over.

Cross-entropy by hand

With the evidence sat 8, ran 3, slept 1 and a model that guesses sat 55%, ran 30%, slept 15%, the surprise at each real word is -log2(model probability), weighted by how often that word actually happened:

P(sat) = 8/12 = 0.67, P(ran) = 3/12 = 0.25, P(slept) = 1/12 = 0.08 cross-entropy = 0.67·(-log2 0.55) + 0.25·(-log2 0.30) + 0.08·(-log2 0.15) ≈ 0.58 + 0.43 + 0.23 = 1.24 bits → perplexity = 2^1.24 ≈ 2.4 words

The live panel recomputes these as you drag. A perfect model would reach the entropy floor of about 1.19 bits; the gap above it is the model's avoidable surprise, and training exists to shrink it.

Quick check: this context honestly allows several next words. As the model learns, its cross-entropy falls - how low can it possibly go?

Probability How much chance the model gives one next word, from 0 to 1.
Conditional probability The chance of a word given the context, like sat given the cat.

Cross-entropy The model's real surprise at the actual next word. Training drives this down.
Perplexity Cross-entropy as a count: about how many next words the model is torn between.

2. Words as vectors

Give each word a few numbers so comparison becomes geometry.

Surprise told us how wrong a guess was. But before the model can guess at all, it needs a way to tell words apart - and, more usefully, to notice when two words are alike. Chapter 2's answer was to hand each word a short list of learned numbers, its vector. Stack all those rows into one table and you have an embedding matrix: one row per word, and "looking up a word" is simply grabbing its row. The row is not a dictionary definition; it is a handle the model can tune. Here we shrink each word down to just two numbers, so we can draw it as an arrow on a grid and actually see what the model sees.

Why arrows? Because the model compares words with one humble operation: the dot product. Multiply the matching numbers of two vectors, add the results, done. If that feels too small to power a language model, good - hold on to the surprise. The payoff is geometric: that single number runs large when two arrows point the same way, near zero when they are unrelated, and negative when they point in opposite directions. Cosine similarity is the same idea stripped down to direction alone, and distance just asks how far apart the tips sit. Hold on to that humble dot product: it is exactly how the model will rank every candidate next word - and, much later, how attention will rank everything.

What the comparison says

dot product

When the model ranks the next word, it compares a context vector against each candidate word vector with exactly this dot product. Bigger score, higher rank. This is why I keep slowing down on the dot product: attention, much later, runs the same comparison at a far larger scale.

One dot product, by hand

Say cat = [0.7, 0.3] and dog = [0.6, 0.4]. Their similarity score is just multiply-and-add:

dot(cat, dog) = 0.7×0.6 + 0.3×0.4 = 0.42 + 0.12 = 0.54 cosine = dot ÷ (length cat × length dog) ≈ 0.54 ÷ (0.76 × 0.72) ≈ 0.98

A cosine near 1 means cat and dog are pointing in almost the same direction, so the model can share what it learns about one with the other. That shared strength is the whole reason Chapter 2 moved from exact counts to learned vectors.

3. Gradients, gradient descent, and loss

When the guess is wrong, which way do we nudge?

We can now measure surprise and compare words - but measuring a mistake is not the same as fixing it. This is the part I like because learning stops feeling mystical and becomes a nudge. Here is the question that actually does the learning: when a guess is wrong, which way, and how far, should we move the numbers? To see the answer with nothing in the way, let's shrink the model to the simplest thing that can possibly learn - a straight line with two knobs, a slope and an intercept - and ask it to pass through a cloud of dots. The loss is just the average squared gap between the line and the dots: one number that answers "how wrong are we right now?". (Our word model will later swap in cross-entropy loss, but the nudging idea is exactly the same.)

Now the move that does all the work. The gradient answers "if I jiggle this one knob, does the loss climb or fall, and how steeply?" - it points straight uphill. So to head downhill, we simply step the opposite way. Gradient descent is nothing grander than repeating that one move: measure the slope, take a small step size downhill, repeat, until the line settles into the dots. Do you already feel why the step size is delicate? Too small and you crawl; too big and you leap clean over the bottom. Press Step descent below and watch the line walk toward the dots, one nudge at a time.

Loss and gradients

mean squared error

One step, by hand

Take a single point (x = 1, y = 1) and a line currently set to slope = 0, intercept = 0, so it predicts 0. The squared-error loss and its gradients are:

error = prediction − y = 0 − 1 = −1, loss = error² = 1 gradient on slope = 2·error·x = 2·(−1)·1 = −2 new slope = slope − stepSize·gradient = 0 − 0.1·(−2) = +0.2

A negative gradient means "go up," so the update nudges the slope upward toward the point. Repeat across all points and the line settles into the cloud. That is the whole trick. Every weight in the next-word model is trained by this same subtraction, just thousands of them at once.

4. Softmax and assigning blame

Turn raw word scores into next-word probabilities.

Here is an awkward gap. The network, once we build it, will not naturally hand us neat percentages. It will hand us a raw score for every word it could predict - a number called a logit. But a logit can be 4.2 or -1.1 or anything at all, and we promised the model would output probabilities: numbers between 0 and 1 that add up to 1. So how do we get from a pile of any-old-numbers to a clean distribution? Softmax is the little bridge in the middle, and it is a two-step trick: exponentiate every score (which makes it positive and stretches the gaps), then divide each by the running total so the whole set sums to 1. Temperature is the dial that stretches or sharpens the result - the very same knob you turned to make Chapter 1's generator bold or cautious.

Now the satisfying part: assigning blame. With probabilities in hand, cross-entropy reads off how much the model put on the word that actually came next - little there means a lot of surprise, a high loss. To fix it, backpropagation hands each word its share of the blame: "you, the word it wrongly favored - too high, push down; you, the true word - too low, push up." And here is the small miracle that makes the whole machine practical: for softmax paired with cross-entropy, that blame collapses to one of the simplest expressions in all of deep learning - probability − target. Predicted minus wanted. Move the raw scores below and watch that one difference become the error signal every weight will follow.

5. Build the forward pass

Stack the pieces into a tiny network we can inspect.

We now have five tools on the bench - probability, vectors, the dot product, softmax, and the gradient nudge. This is the moment they click together into a machine. The smallest part is a neuron: it multiplies its inputs by weights, adds a bias, and runs the result through an activation function. Why bother with the activation? Because without that little bend - a ReLU, a tanh, a sigmoid - a stack of layers can only ever draw a straight line, no matter how tall you build it. The bend is what lets it curve. Line up several neurons and you have a layer; stack the layers and you have a multilayer perceptron, or MLP.

Read the whole forward pass once, top to bottom - it is shorter than it sounds: look up an embedding row for each context word, join the rows, push them through a hidden layer with an activation, and finish with softmax over the candidate words. Context words in, a next-word distribution out. Change the context or the activation below and watch the prediction shift. These weights are fixed and illustrative for now; in the very next section the model learns its own.

This is exactly the shape of the model you are about to train: context words in, a next-word distribution out. The only thing missing is learning, which is the four-beat loop of forward pass, loss, backward pass, and update.

7. Train a next-word model

Now train the real thing: a tiny neural language model.

This is the payoff. Not a fake diagram, not a pretend model: everything above becomes one small network that predicts the next word, trained right here on a tiny corpus. It is a miniature of the neural language model Bengio and colleagues introduced in 2003: each context word is looked up in an embedding table, the rows are joined and pushed through a hidden layer with a tanh activation, and softmax produces a probability for every word in the vocabulary. Cross-entropy scores each guess and backprop nudges every weight, including the embedding rows themselves.

Press Train and watch three things move together: the loss falls, perplexity drops from "torn between many words" toward "a confident handful," and the prediction bars for a context like the cat sharpen onto the words that really follow it. Then generate a sentence and see the same math from Chapter 1, sampling one word at a time, but now driven by learned vectors instead of raw counts.

Take a moment to appreciate what just happened. With nothing but probability, vectors, a dot product, softmax, and a gradient nudge, a pile of numbers taught itself to finish sentences. That is the whole engine of a modern LLM, only tiny. It still has one real limit, though: it reads a fixed two-word window and learns every context on its own, much like the n-grams back in Chapter 1. We already pinned down the raw text-to-token step in Chapter 3. Next we meet the Transformer, the architecture that finally lets a model look across a whole sentence at once.

8. Going deeper

Further reading.

If the live examples now feel comfortable, I would go deeper in this order: first the paper behind the little model you just trained, then the classic sources on backpropagation, softmax, and perplexity. You do not need to read them to keep going, but they are the real trailheads.

  1. Bengio et al., A Neural Probabilistic Language Model The fixed-window neural language model your next-word lab is a miniature of.
  2. Jurafsky and Martin, Chapter 3: N-gram Language Models Entropy, cross-entropy, and perplexity in the language-model setting.
  3. Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, Deep Learning, Chapter 6 A rigorous treatment of deep feedforward networks.
  4. Deep Learning, Chapter 4: Numerical Computation Gradient-based optimization, numerical stability, and softmax details.
  5. Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams, Learning representations by back-propagating errors The classic backpropagation paper.
  6. Christopher Olah, Calculus on Computational Graphs A visual intuition for backpropagation as local gradients moving through a graph.
