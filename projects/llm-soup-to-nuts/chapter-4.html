Learning by doing

The math prerequisites for understanding LLMs in-depth

In this chapter, we will build a tiny neural network, train it start to finish right here in your browser, and watch a pile of numbers learn to finish sentences.

Yes, there is math here. But every concept earns its keep by solving a problem you can actually see. Probability puts a number on how surprised the model is by the real next word. Vectors let it compare words. Loss squeezes a wrong guess into a single number. Gradients influence which way to nudge the numbers so the next guess is a little better. We will progressively build the math to gain a full understanding of how LLMs work under the hood.

We keep the example deliberately tiny, because a tiny model lets you watch the whole loop: words become numbers, numbers become next-word probabilities, a loss is measured, and the parameters shuffle a little closer to right. A modern LLM is a much, much larger version of this exact loop - same four beats, just billions of numbers instead of a handful.

Guiding question: how can a pile of numbers learn to finish your sentence, when nobody wrote the grammar rules by hand? That is the mystery this whole chapter slowly takes apart.

Compare with vectors Give each word numbers, then score candidates with dot products and similarity.
Nudge with gradients Use loss, gradients, gradient descent, softmax, and backprop.

Before we dive in, a few needed prerequisites are in order. This chapter leans on Chapters 1 and 2, and it builds every piece of math slowly, the moment we actually need it. A little comfort with the following will make the ride smoother - use the links as quick refreshers if anything feels new.

  • Chapter 1. We reuse next-word counts turned into probability (P(next | context)) and the idea of perplexity as how "surprised" a model is.
  • Chapter 2. We reuse the big idea that each word is a short row of numbers (a vector), and that a dot product compares two of them.
  • Multiply, add, compare. That is the entire math vocabulary on this page. If you can multiply two numbers and add the results, you can follow every formula here.

1. Surprise and the next word

Start where Chapter 1 left off: how surprised is the model?

One heads-up before we start. The other chapters keep the math light on purpose. This one does the opposite: we are going to write down the equations. Do not let that scare you off. Every symbol gets explained in plain words right where it appears, and every formula is just a tidy way of writing a concept you will have already worked out by hand a line above. Where a piece of calculus shows up, I will define it on . Read the words first and the symbols second, and it will all make sense.

Let's begin with a feeling you already have: surprise. Read "the cat ___" and your mind quietly bets on a couple of words - sat, maybe ran. End the sentence on sat and you feel nothing; end it on helicopter and you feel something is not right. A language model has that exact reflex, and all of training is just teaching the model to get less surprised and more confident at the words that really come next. So our very first job is to put a number on surprise.

To make this concrete, let's start with a small pile of next-word counts for a specific context. Say that after the phrase the cat, we look at our training data and see the word sat 8 times, ran 3 times, and slept once—12 examples in all. (These are round, hand-picked numbers so the math stays clean; the real corpus we actually train on at the end of the chapter is larger and messier, but the core idea is identical.) We can express the chance of sat coming next as P(sat | the cat) = 8 / 12 ≈ 0.67 (or 67%). That vertical bar means "given": we are not asking how common the word sat is everywhere, only how common it is right after the cat. This represents the true distribution of our data.

When training a language model, we want its predictions to match this reality as closely as possible. To measure how well it's doing, we rely on three heavily connected concepts from Information Theory. All three answer one fundamental question: is the model confident, or flailing? Let's look at them through the lens of "surprise."

Entropy is the inherent uncertainty of the language itself. Imagine you knew the exact true probabilities (like our 67%, 25%, and 8%). Even with perfect knowledge, you still can't be 100% sure what the next word will be; there is a baseline level of average surprise when the next word is finally revealed. Entropy calculates this absolute floor, measured in bits. It is the smallest average surprise anyone could manage on this context. If a context forces exactly one specific next word every single time, the entropy is zero bits—no surprise at all.

Cross-entropy, on the other hand, is the model's actual average surprise when it uses its own internal, imperfect probabilities to predict the real next words. If our model incorrectly thinks slept is the most likely next word, it will incur a massive "surprise penalty" every time sat repeatedly shows up in the training data. The closer the model's predictions match the true distribution, the lower the cross-entropy gets, eventually bottoming out exactly at the true Entropy floor. In machine learning, minimizing cross-entropy is the standard way we teach models to predict text.

Finally, perplexity rewrites this cross-entropy metric on a much friendlier, intuitive scale. While "bits of surprise" can feel abstract, perplexity translates those bits back into an effective vocabulary size. Mathematically, it is simply the exponentiation of cross-entropy (e.g., 2cross-entropy). If a model has a perplexity of 4 on a given text, it means that, on average, the model felt as confused as if it were choosing blindly between 4 equally likely words. A perfect model that knows exactly what comes next has a perplexity of 1.

Now feel it for yourself. The left sliders are the evidence - how often each word actually followed the context. The right sliders are the model's guesses. Drag a guess away from the evidence and watch cross-entropy climb; drag it back and watch the surprise fall toward the entropy floor. That falling number is exactly what training will do for us, automatically, thousands of times over.

With the evidence sat 8, ran 3, slept 1 and a model that guesses sat 55% (0.55), ran 30% (0.30), slept 15% (0.15), the surprise at each real word is -log2(model probability), weighted by how often that word actually happened:

P(sat) = 8/12 ≈ 0.67, P(ran) = 3/12 = 0.25, P(slept) = 1/12 ≈ 0.083 cross-entropy = 0.67·(-log2 0.55) + 0.25·(-log2 0.30) + 0.083·(-log2 0.15) ≈ 0.58 + 0.43 + 0.23 = 1.24 bits → perplexity = 2^1.24 ≈ 2.4 words

The live panel recomputes these as you drag. A perfect model would reach the entropy floor of about 1.19 bits; the gap above it is the model's avoidable surprise, and training exists to shrink it.

The same idea, in symbols

Everything we just discussed has a compact mathematical form. The core building block is the surprise of a single word. If our model assigns a probability \(q\) to a specific word, the surprise of seeing that word is measured as \(-\log_2(q)\) bits. Notice how this aligns with our intuition: if the model is highly confident (\(q\) is close to 1), the surprise drops to nearly zero. But if the model thinks a word is almost impossible (\(q\) is close to 0), the resulting surprise penalty is massive.

To evaluate the model's overall performance, we don't just look at one word; we calculate the expected (or average) surprise. We take the surprise of every possible next word and weight it by how often that word truly appears in the evidence (\(p\)). Adding all these weighted surprises together gives us the Entropy (if using the true probabilities \(p\) for the surprise) or Cross-Entropy (if using the model's imperfect guesses \(q\) for the surprise):

$$H(p) = -\sum_{w} p(w)\,\log_2 p(w) \qquad H(p,q) = -\sum_{w} p(w)\,\log_2 q(w)$$ $$\text{Perplexity} = 2^{H(p,q)}$$
  • \(p(w)\): The true distribution — how often word \(w\) actually follows this context in the data.
  • \(q(w)\): The model's prediction — the probability the model assigns to word \(w\).
  • \(\sum_{w}\): The sum over the entire vocabulary. We add up the probability-weighted surprise for every candidate next word.
  • \(\log_2\): Measures the surprise in bits, framing the prediction as binary choices. Perplexity turns those abstract bits back into an intuitive word count.

Checking the math: Let's plug in the numbers from our live panel. Our true data is \(p = (\tfrac{8}{12}, \tfrac{3}{12}, \tfrac{1}{12})\) and our model's guesses are \(q = (0.55, 0.30, 0.15)\).

The true Entropy floor is \(H(p) = 1.19\) bits. The model's Cross-Entropy is \(H(p,q) = 1.24\) bits, and its Perplexity is \(2^{1.24} \approx 2.36\) words.

A note on actual training: Later sections (and ML frameworks like PyTorch) switch to using the natural log \(\ln\). This measures the exact same surprise, but in nats instead of bits. In that environment, cross-entropy is often just called your "Loss," and perplexity is calculated as \(e^{\text{loss}}\).

Quick check: this context honestly allows several next words. As the model learns, its cross-entropy falls - how low can it possibly go?

Why \(-\log_2(q)\)? The Four Rules of Surprise

The formula \(-\log_2(q)\) wasn't chosen randomly. Claude Shannon formulated it in 1948 because it is the only mathematical function that perfectly matches how our common-sense concept of "surprise" works:

  • Certainty means zero surprise: If a model is 100% certain (probability \(q = 1.0\)), it shouldn't be surprised when it's right. Mathematically, \(-\log_2(1) = 0\) bits.
  • Surprises must add up: If you flip a coin (50% chance) and draw a specific suit from a deck of cards (25% chance), the probability of both happening is found by multiplying (\(0.5 \times 0.25 = 0.125\)). But surprise shouldn't multiply; it should add. Logarithms are the exact mathematical tool that turns multiplication into addition.
  • We want positive numbers: Probabilities are always fractions, and logarithms of fractions are negative (e.g., \(\log_2(0.5) = -1\)). Shannon simply added a negative sign to the front of the equation to flip the result back to a positive number, giving us \(1\) bit of surprise.
  • We want to measure in "Choices": Using base-2 (\(\log_2\)) ties the math directly to binary choices, or bits. A 1-in-2 chance (\(q = 0.5\)) yields \(1\) bit. A 1-in-4 chance yields \(2\) bits. As the probability approaches zero, the surprise shoots toward infinity.

The Deeper Truth: Optimal Data Compression

To get to the next level of depth, we have to look past the metaphor of "surprise." Shannon was originally trying to figure out the fundamental physical limits of data compression. The reason \(-\log_2(q)\) is the "right" formula is because it calculates the absolute minimum number of 1s and 0s (bits) required to encode a piece of information.

Imagine playing a game of 20 Questions to guess the next word, where every yes/no question costs exactly 1 bit. To be efficient, you assign your shortest paths (fewest questions) to the most common words:

  • If a word happens 50% of the time (1-in-2), it takes exactly 1 question to identify (\(-\log_2(0.5) = 1\) bit).
  • If a word happens 25% of the time (1-in-4), it takes 2 questions (\(-\log_2(0.25) = 2\) bits).
  • If a word happens 12.5% of the time (1-in-8), it takes 3 questions (\(-\log_2(0.125) = 3\) bits).

The formula isn't an arbitrary penalty; it is the literal number of bits you must spend to encode that word in a perfectly optimized system.

Why Cross-Entropy is the Perfect Loss Function

When we evaluate a Large Language Model, we are judging the "rulebook" it uses to compress and predict language.

Entropy (\(H\)) is the unbreakable law of physics for compression. If we know the exact true probabilities of every word, Entropy tells us the minimum average number of bits per word needed to encode the text.

Cross-Entropy measures what happens when we compress the true text using our model's flawed rulebook. If our model incorrectly predicts that a common word like sat is incredibly rare (say, \(q = 0.01\)), it will assign a long, 6.6-bit code to it (\(-\log_2(0.01) \approx 6.6\)). Because sat actually shows up all the time, we are forced to constantly use this massive 6.6-bit code, wasting a huge amount of data.

When we train an LLM to "minimize cross-entropy loss," we are literally forcing it to rearrange its internal probabilities so its coding rulebook wastes as few bits as possible compared to the true distribution of the text.

2. Words as vectors

Representing words as vectors - a list of numbers.

We used surprise to tell us how wrong our guess was. But before the model can guess at all, it needs a way to tell words apart - and, more usefully, to notice when two words are alike. You already do this without thinking: cat and dog feel like cousins, while cat and table feel like strangers. We want to give the model that same gut feeling, using nothing but numbers. Chapter 2's answer was to hand each word a short list of learned numbers, its vector. Stack all those rows into one table and you have an embedding matrix: one row per word, and "looking up a word" is simply grabbing its row. The row is not a dictionary definition; it is something that the learning algorithm can tune. Here we shrink each word down to just two numbers, so we can draw it as an arrow on a grid and actually see what the model sees.

Why arrows? Because the model compares words with one humble operation: the dot product. Multiply the matching numbers of two vectors, add the results, done. If that feels too small to power a language model, good - hold on to the surprise. The payoff is geometric: that single number runs large when two arrows point the same way, near zero when they are unrelated, and negative when they point in opposite directions. Cosine similarity is the same idea stripped down to direction alone, and distance just asks how far apart the tips sit. Hold on to that humble dot product: it is exactly how the model will rank every candidate next word - and, much later, how attention will rank everything.

What the comparison says

dot product

When the model ranks the next word, it compares a context vector against each candidate word vector with exactly this dot product. Bigger score, higher rank. This is why I keep slowing down on the dot product: attention, much later, runs the same comparison at a far larger scale.

One dot product, by hand

Say cat = [0.7, 0.3] and dog = [0.6, 0.4]. Their similarity score is just multiply-and-add:

dot(cat, dog) = 0.7×0.6 + 0.3×0.4 = 0.42 + 0.12 = 0.54 cosine = dot ÷ (length cat × length dog) ≈ 0.54 ÷ (0.76 × 0.72) ≈ 0.98

A cosine near 1 means cat and dog are pointing in almost the same direction, so the model can share what it learns about one with the other. That shared strength is the whole reason Chapter 2 moved from exact counts to learned vectors.

The same comparison, in symbols

Why does multiplying and adding numbers measure meaning? Imagine each dimension in a word vector represents a hidden trait (like "furriness" or "royalty"). When you multiply matching components, you are checking for shared traits. If both cat and dog score high on "furry," multiplying those values produces a large number. Adding these up across all dimensions yields the dot product—a raw score of semantic overlap.

This mathematical trick wasn't invented for language. In the 1880s, physicists like Josiah Willard Gibbs formulated vector algebra to calculate how much of one physical force aligns with another (like wind pushing against a sail). They proved that the dot product inherently captures the geometric angle (\(\theta\)) between two arrows.

For language models, this geometric angle is crucial. Raw dot products have a flaw: they grow larger simply if a vector is longer (e.g., if a word appears more frequently during training). By dividing the dot product by the lengths of the two vectors, we strip away the magnitude. We are left only with the direction they point. This is cosine similarity:

$$a \cdot b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \qquad \lVert a \rVert = \sqrt{a \cdot a} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{a \cdot b}{\lVert a \rVert\,\lVert b \rVert}$$
  • \(a_i\): the \(i\)-th number inside vector \(a\) (e.g., its score for a specific trait).
  • \(n\): how many numbers each vector holds (just 2 in this demo, but thousands in an LLM).
  • \(\lVert a \rVert\): the magnitude or overall length of the arrow \(a\).
  • \(\theta\): the angle between the two arrows. If they point the exact same way (identical meaning), the angle is 0°, and \(\cos(0) = 1\).

With \(\text{cat} = [0.7, 0.3]\) and \(\text{dog} = [0.6, 0.4]\): \(a \cdot b = 0.54\), \(\lVert \text{cat} \rVert \approx 0.76\), \(\lVert \text{dog} \rVert \approx 0.72\), so \(\cos\theta \approx 0.98\). The model ranks every candidate next word with this one operation, finding the vector that points in the most similar direction.

3. Gradients, gradient descent, and loss

When the guess is wrong, which way do we nudge?

We can now measure surprise and compare words - but measuring a mistake is not the same as fixing it. This is the part I like because learning stops feeling mystical and becomes a nudge. Here is the question that actually does the learning: when a guess is wrong, which way, and how far, should we move the numbers? To see the answer with nothing in the way, let's shrink the model to the simplest thing that can possibly learn - a straight line with two knobs, a slope and an intercept - and ask it to pass through a cloud of dots. The loss is just the average squared gap between the line and the dots: one number that answers "how wrong are we right now?". (Our word model will later swap in cross-entropy loss, but the nudging idea is exactly the same.)

Now the move that does all the work. The gradient answers "if I jiggle this one knob, does the loss climb or fall, and how steeply?" - it points straight uphill. So to head downhill, we simply step the opposite way. Gradient descent is nothing grander than repeating that one move: measure the slope, take a small step size downhill, repeat, until the line settles into the dots. Do you already feel why the step size is delicate? Too small and you crawl; too big and you leap clean over the bottom. Press Step descent below and watch the line walk toward the dots, one nudge at a time.

Loss and gradients

mean squared error

One step, by hand

Take a single point (x = 1, y = 1) and a line currently set to slope = 0, intercept = 0, so it predicts 0. The squared-error loss and its gradients are:

error = prediction − y = 0 − 1 = −1, loss = error² = 1 gradient on slope = 2·error·x = 2·(−1)·1 = −2 new slope = slope − stepSize·gradient = 0 − 0.1·(−2) = +0.2

A negative gradient means "go up," so the update nudges the slope upward toward the point. Repeat across all points and the line settles into the cloud. That is the whole trick. Every weight in the next-word model is trained by this same subtraction, just thousands of them at once.

Loss, gradient, and the update rule, in symbols

The loss is the average squared gap between the line's prediction \(\hat{y}\) and the true value \(y\). The gradient \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) reads as "how fast does the loss \(L\) change if I nudge just the one knob \(w\), holding the rest still." That is all the partial derivative \(\partial\) means here:

$$L = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\big(\hat{y}_i - y_i\big)^2$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial\,\text{slope}} = 2(\hat{y}-y)\,x \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial\,\text{intercept}} = 2(\hat{y}-y)$$ $$w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}$$
  • \(\hat{y}\): the line's prediction, \(\text{slope}\cdot x + \text{intercept}\); \(y\): the true value.
  • \(N\): how many points we average the loss over.
  • \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\): the gradient, the uphill slope of the loss for knob \(w\).
  • \(\eta\) (eta): the learning rate, the step size; the same slider in the lab.
  • \(\leftarrow\): "becomes," the weight is replaced by its nudged value.

One point \((x{=}1,\ y{=}1)\) with \(\text{slope}{=}0\) gives \(\hat{y}=0\) and error \(\hat{y}-y = -1\), so \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial\,\text{slope}} = 2(-1)(1) = -2\), and \(\text{slope} \leftarrow 0 - 0.1(-2) = +0.2\). Subtracting the gradient steps downhill; \(\eta\) sets how big that step is. Too small and you crawl, too big and you leap past the bottom.

4. Softmax and assigning blame

Turn raw word scores into next-word probabilities.

Here is an awkward gap. The network, once we build it, will not naturally hand us neat percentages. It will hand us a raw score for every word it could predict - a number called a logit. But a logit can be 4.2 or -1.1 or anything at all, and we promised the model would output probabilities: numbers between 0 and 1 that add up to 1. So how do we get from a pile of any-old-numbers to a clean distribution? Softmax is the little bridge in the middle, and it is a two-step trick: exponentiate every score (which makes it positive and stretches the gaps), then divide each by the running total so the whole set sums to 1. Temperature is the dial that stretches or sharpens the result - the very same knob you turned to make Chapter 1's generator bold or cautious.

Now the satisfying part: assigning blame. With probabilities in hand, cross-entropy reads off how much the model put on the word that actually came next - little there means a lot of surprise, a high loss. (A quick units note: we counted surprise in bits earlier, using log base 2; the live loss here uses the natural log instead, so it is measured in nats - the same quantity on a different base, which is why perplexity later reads as e^loss rather than 2^loss.) To fix it, backpropagation hands each word its share of the blame: "you, the word it wrongly favored - too high, push down; you, the true word - too low, push up." And here is the small miracle that makes the whole machine practical: for softmax paired with cross-entropy, that blame collapses to one of the simplest expressions in all of deep learning - probability − target. Predicted minus wanted. Move the raw scores below and watch that one difference become the error signal every weight will follow.

Take the three raw scores above - sat 1.70, ran 0.80, slept -1.10 at temperature 1. Exponentiate each, then divide by the total so the results sum to 1:

e^1.70 = 5.47, e^0.80 = 2.23, e^-1.10 = 0.33, total = 8.03 softmax: sat = 5.47/8.03 = 0.681, ran = 2.23/8.03 = 0.277, slept = 0.33/8.03 = 0.041 if sat is the true word: loss = -ln(0.681) ≈ 0.38 nats blame = probability − target: sat 0.681−1 = −0.32, ran +0.28, slept +0.04

The live panel recomputes these as you drag the scores. The blame on the true word is negative (its score gets pushed up) while the others are positive (pushed down): that single probability − target vector is the error signal every weight will follow.

Softmax, loss, and blame, in symbols

Softmax exponentiates each raw score \(z_i\) and divides by the total, so the outputs are positive and sum to 1. Temperature \(T\) divides the scores first, stretching or sharpening them. Cross-entropy then reads off the negative log probability of the true word, and the gradient of that loss with respect to each score collapses to one clean difference:

$$q_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} \qquad q_i^{(T)} = \frac{e^{z_i / T}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j / T}}$$ $$L = -\sum_i y_i \log q_i = -\log q_{\text{true}} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = q_i - y_i$$
  • \(z_i\): the raw score (logit) for word \(i\); \(q_i\): its softmax probability.
  • \(y_i\): the target, 1 for the true next word and 0 for every other word.
  • \(T\): temperature; \(T{=}1\) leaves the scores unchanged.
  • \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = q_i - y_i\): the blame, "probability minus target."

With \(z = (1.70,\ 0.80,\ -1.10)\): \(q = (0.68,\ 0.28,\ 0.04)\). If sat is the true word, \(L = -\ln(0.68) \approx 0.38\) nats, and the blame is \((0.68{-}1,\ 0.28,\ 0.04) = (-0.32,\ +0.28,\ +0.04)\): push the true word's score up, the others down.

And backpropagation? That blame vector is the starting point. To reach a weight buried deeper in the network, backprop multiplies the local sensitivities step by step along the path back to that weight. That multiply-as-you-go bookkeeping is just the chain rule from calculus, applied layer by layer, and it hands every weight its own \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) for the update rule from the last section. The visual walk-throughs in the references trace it gradient by gradient.

5. Build the forward pass

Stack the pieces into a tiny network we can inspect.

We now have five tools on the bench - probability, vectors, the dot product, softmax, and the gradient nudge. This is the moment they click together into a machine. The smallest part is a neuron: it multiplies its inputs by weights, adds a bias, and runs the result through an activation function. Picture it as a tiny opinion-maker: it listens to a few numbers, weighs each one by how much it trusts that input, adds its own standing lean (the bias), and then decides how strongly to speak up. Why bother with the activation? Because without that little bend - a ReLU, a tanh, a sigmoid - a stack of layers can only ever draw a straight line, no matter how tall you build it. The bend is what lets it curve. Line up several neurons and you have a layer; stack the layers and you have a multilayer perceptron, or MLP.

Read the whole forward pass once, top to bottom - it is shorter than it sounds: look up an embedding row for each context word, join the rows, push them through a hidden layer with an activation, and finish with softmax over the candidate words. Context words in, a next-word distribution out. Change the context or the activation below and watch the prediction shift. These weights are fixed and illustrative for now; in the very next section the model learns its own.

Each context word is an index into the embedding table; its row is a parameter the network can tune. Read the steps top to bottom: two lookups, one activated hidden layer, raw logits, then softmax probabilities.

This is exactly the shape of the model you are about to train: context words in, a next-word distribution out. The only thing missing is learning, which is the four-beat loop of forward pass, loss, backward pass, and update.

The whole forward pass, in symbols

A single neuron takes its inputs \(x\), weights them, adds a bias, and runs the result through an activation \(f\):

$$a = f(w \cdot x + b)$$

Stack those into the tiny next-word network. Look up an embedding row for each of the two context words and join them, pass through one hidden layer with a \(\tanh\) activation, produce one raw score per vocabulary word, and finish with softmax:

$$e = \big[\,E_{w_1}\,;\,E_{w_2}\,\big] \qquad h = \tanh(W_1 e + b_1)$$ $$z = W_2 h + b_2 \qquad p = \text{softmax}(z)$$
  • \(E_{w}\): the embedding row looked up for word \(w\); \(e\): the two rows joined.
  • \(W_1, b_1\): the hidden layer's weights and bias; \(\tanh\): its activation \(f\).
  • \(W_2, b_2\): the output weights and bias that turn the hidden layer into logits \(z\).
  • \(p\): the next-word probability for every word in the vocabulary.

This is exactly the network in the lab two sections down. Training learns \(E\), \(W_1\), \(W_2\), \(b_1\), and \(b_2\) by running this forward pass, scoring it with cross-entropy, and applying \(w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) to every weight at once.

7. Train a next-word model

Now train the real thing: a tiny neural language model.

This is the payoff. Not a fake diagram, not a pretend model: everything above becomes one small network that predicts the next word, trained right here on a tiny corpus. It is a miniature of the neural language model Bengio and colleagues introduced in 2003: each context word is looked up in an embedding table, the rows are joined and pushed through a hidden layer with a tanh activation, and softmax produces a probability for every word in the vocabulary. Cross-entropy scores each guess and backprop nudges every weight, including the embedding rows themselves.

Press Train and watch three things move together: the loss falls, perplexity drops from "torn between many words" toward "a confident handful," and the prediction bars for a context like the cat sharpen onto the words that really follow it. Then generate a sentence and see the same math from Chapter 1, sampling one word at a time, but now driven by learned vectors instead of raw counts.

Take a moment to appreciate what just happened. With nothing but probability, vectors, a dot product, softmax, and a gradient nudge, a pile of numbers taught itself to finish sentences. That is the whole engine of a modern LLM, only tiny. It still has one real limit, though: it reads a fixed two-word window and learns every context on its own, much like the n-grams back in Chapter 1. 8. Going deeper

Further reading.

If the live examples now feel comfortable, I would go deeper in this order: first the paper behind the little model you just trained, then the classic sources on backpropagation, softmax, and perplexity. You do not need to read them to keep going, but they are the real trailheads.

  1. Bengio et al., A Neural Probabilistic Language Model The fixed-window neural language model your next-word lab is a miniature of.
  2. Jurafsky and Martin, Chapter 3: N-gram Language Models Entropy, cross-entropy, and perplexity in the language-model setting.
  3. Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, Deep Learning, Chapter 6 A rigorous treatment of deep feedforward networks.
  4. Deep Learning, Chapter 4: Numerical Computation Gradient-based optimization, numerical stability, and softmax details.
  5. Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams, Learning representations by back-propagating errors The classic backpropagation paper.
  6. Christopher Olah, Calculus on Computational Graphs A visual intuition for backpropagation as local gradients moving through a graph.
  7. CS231n Neural Network Case Study A readable from-scratch classifier with softmax loss and backpropagation.
