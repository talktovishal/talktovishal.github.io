Learning by doing

The math prerequisites for understanding LLMs in-depth

In this chapter, we will build a tiny neural network, train it start to finish right here in your browser, and watch a pile of numbers learn to finish sentences.

Yes, there is math here. But every concept earns its keep by solving a problem you can actually see. Probability puts a number on how surprised the model is by the real next word. Vectors let it compare words. Loss squeezes a wrong guess into a single number. Gradients influence which way to nudge the numbers so the next guess is a little better. We will progressively build the math to gain a full understanding of how LLMs work under the hood.

We keep the example deliberately tiny, because a tiny model lets you watch the whole loop: words become numbers, numbers become next-word probabilities, a loss is measured, and the parameters shuffle a little closer to right. A modern LLM is a much, much larger version of this exact loop - same four beats, just billions of numbers instead of a handful.

Guiding question: how can a pile of numbers learn to finish your sentence, when nobody wrote the grammar rules by hand? That is the mystery this whole chapter slowly takes apart.

Nudge with gradients Use loss, gradients, gradient descent, softmax, and backprop.

Before we dive in, a few needed prerequisites are in order. This chapter leans on Chapters 1 and 2, and it builds every piece of math slowly, the moment we actually need it. A little comfort with the following will make the ride smoother - use the links as quick refreshers if anything feels new.

  • Chapter 1. We reuse next-word counts turned into probability (P(next | context)) and the idea of perplexity as how "surprised" a model is.

1. Surprise and the next word

Start where Chapter 1 left off: how surprised is the model?

One heads-up before we start. The other chapters keep the math light on purpose. This one does the opposite: we are going to write down the equations. Do not let that scare you off. Every symbol gets explained in plain words right where it appears, and every formula is just a tidy way of writing a concept you will have already worked out by hand a line above. Where a piece of calculus shows up, I will define it on . Read the words first and the symbols second, and it will all make sense.

Let's begin with a feeling you already have: surprise. Read "the cat ___" and your mind quietly bets on a couple of words - sat, maybe ran. End the sentence on sat and you feel nothing; end it on helicopter and you feel something is not right. A language model has that exact reflex, and all of training is just teaching the model to get less surprised and more confident at the words that really come next. So our very first job is to put a number on surprise.

To make this concrete, let's start with a small pile of next-word counts for a specific context. Say that after the phrase the cat, we look at our training data and see the word sat 8 times, ran 3 times, and slept once—12 examples in all. (These are round, hand-picked numbers so the math stays clean; the real corpus we actually train on at the end of the chapter is larger and messier, but the core idea is identical.) We can express the chance of sat coming next as P(sat | the cat) = 8 / 12 ≈ 0.67 (or 67%). That vertical bar means "given": we are not asking how common the word sat is everywhere, only how common it is right after the cat. This represents the true distribution of our data.

When training a language model, we want its predictions to match this reality as closely as possible. To measure how well it's doing, we rely on three heavily connected concepts from Information Theory. All three answer one fundamental question: is the model confident, or flailing? Let's look at them through the lens of "surprise."

Entropy is the inherent uncertainty of the language itself. Imagine you knew the exact true probabilities (like our 67%, 25%, and 8%). Even with perfect knowledge, you still can't be 100% sure what the next word will be; there is a baseline level of average surprise when the next word is finally revealed. Entropy calculates this absolute floor, measured in bits. It is the smallest average surprise anyone could manage on this context. If a context forces exactly one specific next word every single time, the entropy is zero bits—no surprise at all.

Cross-entropy, on the other hand, is the model's actual average surprise when it uses its own internal, imperfect probabilities to predict the real next words. If our model incorrectly thinks slept is the most likely next word, it will incur a massive "surprise penalty" every time sat repeatedly shows up in the training data. The closer the model's predictions match the true distribution, the lower the cross-entropy gets, eventually bottoming out exactly at the true Entropy floor. In machine learning, minimizing cross-entropy is the standard way we teach models to predict text.

Finally, perplexity rewrites this cross-entropy metric on a much friendlier, intuitive scale. While "bits of surprise" can feel abstract, perplexity translates those bits back into an effective vocabulary size. Mathematically, it is simply the exponentiation of cross-entropy (e.g., 2cross-entropy). If a model has a perplexity of 4 on a given text, it means that, on average, the model felt as confused as if it were choosing blindly between 4 equally likely words. A perfect model that knows exactly what comes next has a perplexity of 1.

Now feel it for yourself. The left sliders are the evidence - how often each word actually followed the context. The right sliders are the model's guesses. Drag a guess away from the evidence and watch cross-entropy climb; drag it back and watch the surprise fall toward the entropy floor. That falling number is exactly what training will do for us, automatically, thousands of times over.

With the evidence sat 8, ran 3, slept 1 and a model that guesses sat 55% (0.55), ran 30% (0.30), slept 15% (0.15), the surprise at each real word is -log2(model probability), weighted by how often that word actually happened:

The same idea, in symbols

Everything we just discussed has a compact mathematical form. The core building block is the surprise of a single word. If our model assigns a probability \(q\) to a specific word, the surprise of seeing that word is measured as \(-\log_2(q)\) bits. Notice how this aligns with our intuition: if the model is highly confident (\(q\) is close to 1), the surprise drops to nearly zero. But if the model thinks a word is almost impossible (\(q\) is close to 0), the resulting surprise penalty is massive.

To evaluate the model's overall performance, we don't just look at one word; we calculate the expected (or average) surprise. We take the surprise of every possible next word and weight it by how often that word truly appears in the evidence (\(p\)). Adding all these weighted surprises together gives us the Entropy (if using the true probabilities \(p\) for the surprise) or Cross-Entropy (if using the model's imperfect guesses \(q\) for the surprise):

$$H(p) = -\sum_{w} p(w)\,\log_2 p(w) \qquad H(p,q) = -\sum_{w} p(w)\,\log_2 q(w)$$ $$\text{Perplexity} = 2^{H(p,q)}$$
  • \(p(w)\): The true distribution — how often word \(w\) actually follows this context in the data.
  • \(q(w)\): The model's prediction — the probability the model assigns to word \(w\).
  • \(\sum_{w}\): The sum over the entire vocabulary. We add up the probability-weighted surprise for every candidate next word.
  • \(\log_2\): Measures the surprise in bits, framing the prediction as binary choices. Perplexity turns those abstract bits back into an intuitive word count.

Checking the math: Let's plug in the numbers from our live panel. Our true data is \(p = (\tfrac{8}{12}, \tfrac{3}{12}, \tfrac{1}{12})\) and our model's guesses are \(q = (0.55, 0.30, 0.15)\).

The true Entropy floor is \(H(p) = 1.19\) bits. The model's Cross-Entropy is \(H(p,q) = 1.24\) bits, and its Perplexity is \(2^{1.24} \approx 2.36\) words.

A note on actual training: Later sections (and ML frameworks like PyTorch) switch to using the natural log \(\ln\). This measures the exact same surprise, but in nats instead of bits. In that environment, cross-entropy is often just called your "Loss," and perplexity is calculated as \(e^{\text{loss}}\).

Quick check: this context honestly allows several next words. As the model learns, its cross-entropy falls - how low can it possibly go?

Why \(-\log_2(q)\)? The Four Rules of Surprise

The formula \(-\log_2(q)\) wasn't chosen randomly. Claude Shannon formulated it in 1948 because it is the only mathematical function that perfectly matches how our common-sense concept of "surprise" works:

  • Certainty means zero surprise: If a model is 100% certain (probability \(q = 1.0\)), it shouldn't be surprised when it's right. Mathematically, \(-\log_2(1) = 0\) bits.
  • Surprises must add up: If you flip a coin (50% chance) and draw a specific suit from a deck of cards (25% chance), the probability of both happening is found by multiplying (\(0.5 \times 0.25 = 0.125\)). But surprise shouldn't multiply; it should add. Logarithms are the exact mathematical tool that turns multiplication into addition.
  • We want positive numbers: Probabilities are always fractions, and logarithms of fractions are negative (e.g., \(\log_2(0.5) = -1\)). Shannon simply added a negative sign to the front of the equation to flip the result back to a positive number, giving us \(1\) bit of surprise.
  • We want to measure in "Choices": Using base-2 (\(\log_2\)) ties the math directly to binary choices, or bits. A 1-in-2 chance (\(q = 0.5\)) yields \(1\) bit. A 1-in-4 chance yields \(2\) bits. As the probability approaches zero, the surprise shoots toward infinity.

The Deeper Truth: Optimal Data Compression

To get to the next level of depth, we have to look past the metaphor of "surprise." Shannon was originally trying to figure out the fundamental physical limits of data compression. The reason \(-\log_2(q)\) is the "right" formula is because it calculates the absolute minimum number of 1s and 0s (bits) required to encode a piece of information.

Imagine playing a game of 20 Questions to guess the next word, where every yes/no question costs exactly 1 bit. To be efficient, you assign your shortest paths (fewest questions) to the most common words:

  • If a word happens 50% of the time (1-in-2), it takes exactly 1 question to identify (\(-\log_2(0.5) = 1\) bit).
  • If a word happens 25% of the time (1-in-4), it takes 2 questions (\(-\log_2(0.25) = 2\) bits).
  • If a word happens 12.5% of the time (1-in-8), it takes 3 questions (\(-\log_2(0.125) = 3\) bits).

The formula isn't an arbitrary penalty; it is the literal number of bits you must spend to encode that word in a perfectly optimized system.

Why Cross-Entropy is the Perfect Loss Function

When we evaluate a Large Language Model, we are judging the "rulebook" it uses to compress and predict language.

Entropy (\(H\)) is the unbreakable law of physics for compression. If we know the exact true probabilities of every word, Entropy tells us the minimum average number of bits per word needed to encode the text.

Cross-Entropy measures what happens when we compress the true text using our model's flawed rulebook. If our model incorrectly predicts that a common word like sat is incredibly rare (say, \(q = 0.01\)), it will assign a long, 6.6-bit code to it (\(-\log_2(0.01) \approx 6.6\)). Because sat actually shows up all the time, we are forced to constantly use this massive 6.6-bit code, wasting a huge amount of data.

When we train an LLM to "minimize cross-entropy loss," we are literally forcing it to rearrange its internal probabilities so its coding rulebook wastes as few bits as possible compared to the true distribution of the text.

2. Words as vectors

Representing words as vectors - a list of numbers.

We used surprise to tell us how wrong our guess was. But before the model can guess at all, it needs a way to tell words apart - and, more usefully, to notice when two words are alike. You already do this without thinking: cat and dog feel like cousins, while cat and table feel like strangers. We want to give the model that same gut feeling, using nothing but numbers. Chapter 2's answer was to hand each word a short list of learned numbers, its vector. Stack all those rows into one table and you have an embedding matrix: one row per word, and "looking up a word" is simply grabbing its row. The row is not a dictionary definition; it is something that the learning algorithm can tune. Here we shrink each word down to just two numbers, so we can draw it as an arrow on a grid and actually see what the model sees.

Why arrows? Because the model compares words with one humble operation: the dot product. Multiply the matching numbers of two vectors, add the results, done. If that feels too small to power a language model, good - hold on to the surprise. The payoff is geometric: that single number runs large when two arrows point the same way, near zero when they are unrelated, and negative when they point in opposite directions. Cosine similarity is the same idea stripped down to direction alone, and distance just asks how far apart the tips sit. Hold on to that humble dot product: it is exactly how the model will rank every candidate next word - and, much later, how attention will rank everything.

What the comparison says

dot product

When the model ranks the next word, it compares a context vector against each candidate word vector with exactly this dot product. Bigger score, higher rank. This is why I keep slowing down on the dot product: attention, much later, runs the same comparison at a far larger scale.

One dot product, by hand

Say cat = [0.7, 0.3] and dog = [0.6, 0.4]. Their similarity score is just multiply-and-add:

dot(cat, dog) = 0.7×0.6 + 0.3×0.4 = 0.42 + 0.12 = 0.54 cosine = dot ÷ (length cat × length dog) ≈ 0.54 ÷ (0.76 × 0.72) ≈ 0.98

A cosine near 1 means cat and dog are pointing in almost the same direction, so the model can share what it learns about one with the other. That shared strength is the whole reason Chapter 2 moved from exact counts to learned vectors.

The same comparison, in symbols

Why does multiplying and adding numbers measure meaning? Imagine each dimension in a word vector represents a hidden trait (like "furriness" or "royalty"). When you multiply matching components, you are checking for shared traits. If both cat and dog score high on "furry," multiplying those values produces a large number. Adding these up across all dimensions yields the dot product—a raw score of semantic overlap.

This mathematical trick wasn't invented for language. In the 1880s, physicists like Josiah Willard Gibbs formulated vector algebra to calculate how much of one physical force aligns with another (like wind pushing against a sail). They proved that the dot product inherently captures the geometric angle (\(\theta\)) between two arrows.

For language models, this geometric angle is crucial. Raw dot products have a flaw: they grow larger simply if a vector is longer (e.g., if a word appears more frequently during training). By dividing the dot product by the lengths of the two vectors, we strip away the magnitude. We are left only with the direction they point. This is cosine similarity:

$$a \cdot b = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \qquad \lVert a \rVert = \sqrt{a \cdot a} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{a \cdot b}{\lVert a \rVert\,\lVert b \rVert}$$
  • \(a_i\): the \(i\)-th number inside vector \(a\) (e.g., its score for a specific trait).
  • \(n\): how many numbers each vector holds (just 2 in this demo, but thousands in an LLM).
  • \(\lVert a \rVert\): the magnitude or overall length of the arrow \(a\).
  • \(\theta\): the angle between the two arrows. If they point the exact same way (identical meaning), the angle is 0°, and \(\cos(0) = 1\).

With \(\text{cat} = [0.7, 0.3]\) and \(\text{dog} = [0.6, 0.4]\): \(a \cdot b = 0.54\), \(\lVert \text{cat} \rVert \approx 0.76\), \(\lVert \text{dog} \rVert \approx 0.72\), so \(\cos\theta \approx 0.98\). The model ranks every candidate next word with this one operation, finding the vector that points in the most similar direction.

3. Gradients, gradient descent, and loss

When the guess is wrong, which way do we nudge?

We can now measure surprise and compare words - but measuring a mistake is not the same as fixing it. Here is the question that actually does the learning: when a guess is wrong, which way, and how far, should we move the numbers? Let's start simple, we will shrink the model to the simplest thing that can possibly learn - a straight line with two knobs, a slope and an intercept - and ask it to pass through a cloud of dots. The loss is just the average squared gap between the line and the dots: one number that answers "how wrong are we right now?". (Our word model will later swap in cross-entropy loss, but the nudging idea is exactly the same.)

Now the move that does all the work. Imagine you are standing blindfolded on the side of a hilly valley, trying to reach the absolute bottom (where your loss is zero). Because you can't see the whole landscape, you just feel the angle of the ground directly under your feet. The gradient is the math calculating that slope. It answers "if I jiggle this one knob, does the loss climb or fall, and how steeply?" By mathematical definition, a gradient always points toward the direction of steepest increase—straight uphill. So, to head downhill toward our goal, we take that exact measurement and deliberately step in the opposite direction.

Gradient descent is nothing grander than repeating that one blindfolded move: measure the slope, take a downhill step, repeat, until the line settles perfectly into the dots. This is where the step size (often called the learning rate) becomes incredibly delicate. If your step size is too tiny, you merely crawl, taking an eternity to reach the bottom. But if your step size is too massive, you leap clean over the bottom of the valley, landing even higher up on the opposite wall and making your mistake worse. Press Step descent below and watch the line walk toward the dots, one calculated nudge at a time.

Loss, gradient, and the update rule, in symbols

The loss is the average squared gap between the line's prediction \(\hat{y}\) and the true value \(y\). To fix a bad prediction, we need to know how tweaking our knobs affects that loss. In machine learning, we group all adjustable knobs (like our slope and our intercept) under the universal label of weights, represented by the letter \(w\). Think of \(w\) as a generic blank space: the update rule at the bottom is a template. You run the template once substituting \(w\) for the slope, and then you run it again substituting \(w\) for the intercept.

The gradient \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) reads as "how fast does the loss \(L\) change if I nudge just this one specific weight \(w\), holding the rest perfectly still." That is all the partial derivative \(\partial\) symbol means: isolating one knob at a time.

Calculus Refreshers: If the \(\partial\) symbol looks unfamiliar, Wikipedia has excellent, visual primers on Partial derivatives and the Chain Rule.

How do we calculate that exact gradient number? We use the Chain Rule. Working from the outside in: the \(2\) from the squared error drops down to the front, and then we multiply by the inner variable attached to our knob. Because our prediction is \((\text{slope} \cdot x + \text{intercept})\), the variable attached to the slope is \(x\). The variable attached to the intercept is just \(1\).

$$L = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\big(\hat{y}_i - y_i\big)^2 \qquad (\text{where } \hat{y} = \text{slope} \cdot x + \text{intercept})$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial\,\text{slope}} = 2(\hat{y}-y)\,x \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial\,\text{intercept}} = 2(\hat{y}-y)$$ $$w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}$$
  • \(\hat{y}\): the line's prediction; \(y\): the true value in the data.
  • \(N\): how many points we average the loss over.
  • \(w\): a generic "weight"—a placeholder. You swap in either "slope" or "intercept" when applying the math.
  • \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial w}\): the gradient, representing the uphill slope of the loss for that specific weight \(w\).
  • \(\eta\) (eta): the learning rate or step size (the same slider used in the lab).
  • \(\leftarrow\): "becomes" (we replace the old weight with its newly nudged value).

Walking through one step for the slope: For a single data point \((x{=}1,\ y{=}1)\) and a starting \(\text{slope}{=}0\), our prediction is \(\hat{y}=0\). The error is \(\hat{y}-y = -1\).

The gradient for the slope is \(2(-1)(1) = -2\). Now we use the update rule template, swapping \(w\) for the slope: \(\text{slope} \leftarrow 0 - 0.1(-2) = +0.2\). Subtracting the gradient ensures we step downhill, and \(\eta\) (set to \(0.1\) here) scales how big that step is.

Now make it move. That is the whole recipe - a loss, a gradient, and the one update rule. The exercise just below lets you run it yourself: drag the slope, intercept, and step size, press Step descent, and watch this exact loss, gradient, and update step recompute live on real numbers, one nudge at a time. With the math fresh in mind, the numbers in the lab should now read like old friends.

4. Softmax and assigning blame

Turn raw word scores into next-word probabilities.

Once we run the network to get its output, it will not naturally hand us neat percentages for the list of possible words. It will hand us a raw score for every word it could predict - a number called a logit. But a logit can be 4.2 or -1.1 or anything at all. If we have negative logits, the math breaks. We promised the model would output probabilities: positive numbers between 0 and 1 that add up to 1.

Softmax is the elegant bridge that gets us from any-old-numbers to a clean distribution. It relies on a two-step trick: first, it exponentiates every score. This instantly turns every number (even negative ones) positive, and it stretches the gaps between close numbers so a clear winner emerges. Second, it divides each by the running total so the whole set perfectly sums to 1. Temperature is the dial that controls that stretching. Chapter 1 gave you a coarse version of this same choice - top-pick (always take the strongest word) versus sampling (weighted random).Why does one dial do both? Because temperature acts before the exponential: every score is divided by T first. Make T large and you squash all the scores toward zero, so after exponentiating they land near e0 = 1 - nearly equal, a flat tie where even long-shot words get a real chance. Make T small and you stretch the gaps between scores wide open, so the exponential of the biggest one towers over the rest and runs away with almost all the probability. Push T all the way down toward zero and the single largest score wins outright - which is exactly Chapter 1's top-pick, now recovered as one end of a continuous dial.

Now the satisfying part: assigning blame aka error calculation. Remember cross-entropy? Its the measure of the difference between the model's predicted probabilities and the actual outcomes. With probabilities in hand, cross-entropy builds on that to measure how much confidence the model put on the word that actually came next - little there means a lot of surprise, a high loss. To fix it, the model must hand each word its share of the blame. And here is the small miracle that makes the whole machine practical: for softmax paired with cross-entropy, that blame collapses to one of the simplest expressions in all of deep learning - probability − target. Predicted minus wanted. If the model predicts 0.99 for the true word (target 1.0), the blame is -0.01: just a tiny nudge. But if it predicts 0.01, the blame is -0.99: a massive correction.

Why does it land on something so clean? Because the two halves are built from opposite operations that undo each other. Softmax turns a raw score into a probability with an exponential (e to the score), and cross-entropy measures the loss with that exponential's exact mirror image, the natural log (ln). When we ask calculus how the loss shifts as one score moves, the e and the ln cancel and the messy middle terms fall away, leaving nothing but the gap between what the model gave and what we wanted. There is also a tidy intuition for the leftover: the loss only ever wants the true word's probability to climb toward 1 and every other word's to sink toward 0, so the steepest fix is simply how far each probability still sits from its goal - which is exactly probability − target. (The full line-by-line cancellation is worked out in the symbols panel below.) Move the raw scores below and watch that one difference become the exact error signal every weight will follow.

Units note: a nat is just a unit of surprise - the very same idea as a bit, only measured with the natural log (\(\ln\), base \(e\)) instead of base 2, so \(1\) nat \(\approx 1.44\) bits. We counted surprise in bits earlier, using \(\log_2\); the live loss here uses the natural log \(\ln\), so it is measured in nats - the exact same concept, which is why perplexity later reads as \(e^{\text{loss}}\) instead of \(2^{\text{loss}}\).

Softmax, loss, and blame, in symbols

Softmax exponentiates each raw score \(z_i\) and divides by the total, so the outputs are positive and sum to 1. Temperature \(T\) divides the scores first, stretching or sharpening them. Cross-entropy then reads off the negative log probability of the true word, and the gradient of that loss with respect to each score collapses to one clean difference:

$$q_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} \qquad q_i^{(T)} = \frac{e^{z_i / T}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j / T}}$$ $$L = -\sum_i y_i \ln q_i = -\ln q_{\text{true}} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = q_i - y_i$$
  • \(z_i\): the raw score (logit) for word \(i\); \(q_i\): its softmax probability.
  • \(y_i\): the target, 1 for the true next word and 0 for every other word.
  • \(T\): temperature; \(T{=}1\) leaves the scores unchanged.
  • \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = q_i - y_i\): the blame, "probability minus target."

Why does the blame collapse to that?

It looks too clean to be an accident, and it is not one: the \(\ln\) in cross-entropy and the \(e\) in softmax are near-inverses, so they cancel each other's mess. Start from the loss for the true word \(c\), substitute the softmax definition, and split the fraction with \(\ln\tfrac{a}{b} = \ln a - \ln b\):

$$L = -\ln q_c = -\ln \frac{e^{z_c}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} = -z_c + \ln\sum_j e^{z_j}$$

Now take the derivative with respect to any single score \(z_i\). Each of the two pieces has a tidy, intuitive job:

  • \(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z_i}\big(-z_c\big) = -y_i\): the \(-z_c\) term touches only the true word, pushing its score up. It contributes \(-1\) for the true word and \(0\) for every other.
  • \(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z_i}\ln\sum_j e^{z_j} = \dfrac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} = q_i\): the derivative of that running total is the softmax all over again, which pushes every word's score down in proportion to the probability it currently holds.

Add the two together and the whole gradient is just \(q_i - y_i\). In plain words: lift the true word's score, and shave a little off every word in proportion to how much probability it is currently hogging. The size is self-correcting, too: the blame on the true word is \(q_c - 1\), a feather-light nudge when the model is already confident (\(q_c \approx 1\)) but a full \(-1\) shove when it is badly wrong (\(q_c \approx 0\)). Once the model is perfect, every \(q_i - y_i = 0\) and the blame vanishes, which is exactly when learning should stop.

5. Build the forward pass

Stack the pieces into a tiny network we can inspect.

We now have learnt about five tools - probability, vectors, the dot product, softmax, and the gradient. Think of a neural network as a pipeline. The input data (our context words) is the water flowing through the pipes, and the weights are the adjustable valves. The water always flows forward, but the valves decide how much pressure makes it to each possible next-word output.

The smallest valve intersection is a neuron: it multiplies its inputs by weights, adds a bias, and runs the result through an activation function. Picture it as a tiny opinion-maker: it listens to a few numbers, weighs each one by how much it trusts that input, adds its own standing belief (the bias), and then decides how strongly to speak up. Why bother with the activation? Because without that little bend - a ReLU, a \(\tanh\), a sigmoid - a stack of layers can only ever draw a straight line, no matter how tall you build it. The bend or non-linearity is what lets it curve and learn complex patterns. To understand this, consider: stacking two plain multiply-and-add steps just gives you another multiply-and-add. Push x through one layer to get w1x + b1, feed that into a second, and you land on w2(w1x + b1) + b2 - which tidies up into (w2w1)x + (w2b1 + b2), a single weight and a single bias all over again. A hundred such layers collapse back into one straight line. The activation's non-linearity is exactly what breaks that collapse: once each layer can curve, stacking them lets the network trace shapes a straight line never could. Line up several neurons and you have a layer; stack the layers and you have a multilayer perceptron, or MLP.

Thus, the forward pass is the following: look up an embedding row for each context word, join the rows, push them through a hidden layer with an activation, and finish with softmax over the candidate words. Context words in, a next-word distribution out. Try it out by changing the context or the activation below and watch the prediction shift. These weights are fixed and illustrative for now; we will learn to adjust them later.

The whole forward pass, in symbols

A single neuron takes its inputs \(x\), weights them, adds a bias, and runs the result through an activation \(f\):

$$a = f(w \cdot x + b)$$

Stack those into the tiny next-word network. Look up an embedding row for each of the two context words and join them, pass through one hidden layer with a \(\tanh\) activation, produce one raw score per vocabulary word, and finish with softmax:

$$e = \big[\,E_{w_1}\,;\,E_{w_2}\,\big] \qquad h = \tanh(W_1 e + b_1)$$ $$z = W_2 h + b_2 \qquad p = \text{softmax}(z)$$
  • \(E_{w}\): the embedding row looked up for word \(w\); \(e\): the two rows joined.
  • \(W_1, b_1\): the hidden layer's weights and bias; \(\tanh\): its activation \(f\).
  • \(W_2, b_2\): the output weights and bias that turn the hidden layer into logits \(z\).
  • \(p\): the next-word probability for every word in the vocabulary.

6. Backpropagation

Flow the blame backward to fix the weights.

We know our final error—that "probability minus target" small miracle we found in Section 4. But that only tells us how wrong the final scores are. How do we fix the weights buried deep in the hidden layers? Imagine a factory assembly line: the boss at the end yells, "This car is ruined!" The final assembler says, "Don't just blame me, the chassis arrived crooked from the previous station!" To fix the factory, the blame must be passed backward down the line, proportioned by who caused the most error.

This backward flow of blame is Backpropagation. Instead of guessing how to tweak each hidden weight, we use the Chain Rule from calculus to multiply the sensitivities backward. The error at the output tells the hidden layer how it should have behaved, and the hidden layer tells its weights how they need to adjust. This mathematical chain reaction allows the network to efficiently calculate the perfect downhill gradient for every single weight in one graceful sweep.

Passing blame backward, by hand

Suppose our output gradient (the blame) says the final score \(z\) needs to drop by \(0.5\) (so \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = 0.5\)). That score was created by multiplying a hidden neuron's output \(h\) (say, \(0.8\)) by an output weight \(W_{\text{out}}\) (say, \(2.0\)).

\(\text{Output blame: } \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = 0.5\) \(\text{How much did the weight contribute? } \frac{\partial z}{\partial W_{\text{out}}} = h = 0.8\) \(\text{Chain rule for the weight: } \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{\text{out}}} = (0.5) \times (0.8) = 0.4\) \(\text{How much did the hidden neuron contribute? } \frac{\partial z}{\partial h} = W_{\text{out}} = 2.0\) \(\text{Chain rule for the neuron: } \frac{\partial L}{\partial h} = (0.5) \times (2.0) = 1.0\)

The weight gets updated downhill by its gradient (\(0.4\)). The neuron's gradient (\(1.0\)) becomes the new "blame" passed backward to the layer before it. It multiplies as it goes.

The backward pass, in symbols

Backpropagation is the chain rule run in reverse across the whole network. Start with the simplest case - a single hidden weight \(w\) buried two steps below the loss. To learn how nudging it changes \(L\), you multiply the local sensitivity of every step between them:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial w}$$

That is the entire idea, and it scales: every layer receives an incoming gradient (the blame handed down from above), multiplies it by its own local derivative, and passes the result on as the blame for the layer below. Sweep that rule from the loss back to the very first weight and you get, in one pass, the gradient of \(L\) with respect to every parameter at once.

First, the forward pass we are differentiating

Keep these lines from Section 5 in view - the backward pass simply unwinds them one at a time, last line first:

$$e = [\,E_{w_1};E_{w_2}\,] \qquad z^{(1)} = W_1 e + b_1$$ $$h = f(z^{(1)}) \qquad z = W_2 h + b_2$$ $$q = \text{softmax}(z) \qquad L = -\ln q_c$$
  • \(e\): the two context-word embeddings joined into one input vector.
  • \(z^{(1)}, h\): the hidden layer's pre-activation and its activation through \(f\) (ReLU, tanh, sigmoid).
  • \(z, q\): the output logits and the softmax probabilities.
  • \(y\): the one-hot target (a \(1\) at the true word \(c\), \(0\) everywhere else); \(L = -\ln q_c\) is the cross-entropy loss.

The one move, repeated: blame in, local slope, blame out

Write \(\delta\) for the gradient of the loss with respect to a layer's pre-activation - the "blame" arriving at that layer. The whole algorithm is just two rules applied over and over. To push blame down to the previous layer, multiply by the transpose of the weights (\(W^{\top}\)) you used on the way up. To turn blame into a weight gradient, multiply the incoming blame by the signal that flowed out of that weight on the forward pass.

Layer by layer, for our network

Output layer. Section 4 already did the hardest part: softmax paired with cross-entropy collapses the output blame to one clean difference,

$$\delta = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z} = q - y$$

and from that single vector the output weights and bias fall straight out:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_2} = \delta\,h^{\top} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_2} = \delta$$

Read \(\delta\,h^{\top}\) as one sentence: a weight's gradient is how wrong its output was (\(\delta\)) times how active the neuron feeding it was (\(h\)). A weight earns a big update only when both are large - a confident mistake riding on a strongly-firing neuron.

Hand the blame back to the hidden layer. Each hidden neuron is guilty in proportion to the output errors it fed and the weights it used to feed them:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial h} = W_2^{\top}\,\delta$$

Through the activation gate. Multiply element-wise by the local slope of the activation to get the hidden layer's own blame \(\delta^{(1)}\):

$$\delta^{(1)} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial z^{(1)}} = \big(W_2^{\top}\delta\big)\odot f'(z^{(1)})$$

The \(\odot\) is element-wise multiplication, and \(f'(z^{(1)})\) is the gate: where a neuron was saturated or clipped its slope is near zero, so almost no blame passes through. ReLU's gate is fully shut at \(0\) and wide open (slope \(1\)) above it; tanh and sigmoid are always at least partly closed - exactly the behaviour the lab's step 3 reports.

Input weights, the same shape all over again. Blame times the signal that flowed out - here the input \(e\):

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_1} = \delta^{(1)} e^{\top} \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial b_1} = \delta^{(1)}$$ $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial e} = W_1^{\top}\delta^{(1)}$$

That last term, \(\partial L/\partial e\), is the blame that keeps travelling into the embedding rows themselves - which is precisely how the word vectors get trained in the full model at the end of the chapter.

The update rule ties it back to Section 3

Every parameter then takes the same small downhill step we met with gradient descent, each using its own gradient:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\,\nabla_\theta L \qquad \theta \in \{W_1, b_1, W_2, b_2\}$$

\(\eta\) is the learning rate (the slider in the lab above), and \(\nabla_\theta L\) is shorthand for "all the partial derivatives we just collected for \(\theta\)." The lab computes exactly these quantities for your chosen example - press Take a gradient step and watch \(\delta = q - y\) shrink toward zero as \(q\) slides onto the true word.

Why backward, and why it is cheap

Look at how the same forward quantities - \(h\), \(z^{(1)}\), \(e\) - reappear inside the gradients. That is the whole efficiency trick: one forward pass caches those activations, and one backward pass reuses them to produce the gradient of every weight at once. The total cost is about two forward passes, no matter whether the network has a dozen parameters or a hundred billion. Estimating each gradient the naive way - nudge one weight, rerun the whole network, see how the loss moved - would cost a full pass per weight, which is hopeless at scale. This reverse sweep is what people mean by reverse-mode automatic differentiation, the engine inside PyTorch and TensorFlow.

One warning the formulas predict

Because blame is multiplied by a weight transpose and an activation slope at every layer, a deep stack chains many such factors together. If those factors mostly sit below one (sigmoid's slope never exceeds \(0.25\)), the signal shrinks toward nothing on the way back - the vanishing gradient problem - and if they mostly sit above one, it explodes. That single observation, visible right here in the math, is why later architectures lean on ReLU-like activations, normalization, and the residual "skip" connections we will meet in Chapter 5.

Deep Dive: If you want to watch these gradients flow through the network's operations one node at a time, Christopher Olah's classic Calculus on Computational Graphs is the gold-standard explanation.

The dots are split into a training set it learns from and a held-out test set it never sees. Training accuracy shows how well it fits what it studied; test accuracy is the honest check - did it learn the pattern, or just memorize the flashcards?
